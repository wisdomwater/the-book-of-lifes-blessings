## Preface

There are some books that do not merely speak to the mind.
They enter the imagination.

The visions of Hildegard of Bingen are among these.

Across the centuries, her words have continued to burn with strange brilliance—filled with living symbols, cosmic imagery, virtues and vices, angels and elements, fire and light. She saw creation as charged with divine meaning. Nothing was empty. Everything revealed something of God.

Written in the twelfth century, *Liber Vitae Meritorum*—*The Book of Life’s Merits*—emerged from a world shaped deeply by instability, suffering, disease, war, and fear. Medieval Christianity often relied upon vivid images of judgment, torment, and cosmic struggle to awaken the conscience and guide the soul toward repentance. Hildegard wrote into that world with prophetic intensity. Her visions spoke the language her community understood.

And yet beneath the severity of the imagery, I sensed something else.

A longing for restoration.

A desire for humanity to return to harmony with God.

A vision of the soul not merely condemned, but healed.

As I sat with Hildegard’s work, I began asking a quiet question:

What might these visions sound like if spoken again through the language of patient redemptive love?

What if the struggle between virtue and vice were understood not primarily as divine rejection, but as the slow untangling of the human heart?

What if beneath every distortion there remained a deeper longing waiting to be restored?

This book is my attempt to sit with those questions.

*The Book of Life’s Blessings* is not a direct translation of Hildegard’s text. It is a devotional re-visioning. The original work often moves through fear, warning, punishment, and apocalyptic imagery. Here, those same movements have been reframed through contemplation, tenderness, restoration, and light.

The goal was never to erase Hildegard’s voice.

Nor was it to declare her visions wrong.

Her writings served the spiritual needs of her time. They sought to awaken people to the reality of moral formation, the consequences of human action, and the necessity of turning toward God.

I believe our own age carries different wounds.

We live not only with temptation, but with exhaustion.

Not only with pride, but with fragmentation.

Not only with rebellion, but with shame, anxiety, numbness, loneliness, distraction, and spiritual fatigue.

Many people today no longer need to be convinced that darkness exists. They already feel overwhelmed by it.

What they need is the reminder that light is deeper still.

So this work asks:

What if virtue is not merely moral correctness, but wholeness?

What if repentance is not humiliation, but return?

What if God’s work within the soul is patient, restorative, and compassionate?

What if even our disordered desires contain hidden longings for life, belonging, peace, beauty, safety, communion, and love?

In these pages, vice is not glorified, but neither is the soul shamed. The emphasis shifts from punishment toward healing. The images become quieter. The pace slows. The reader is invited not into fear of shadow, but into love of light.

This does not weaken the seriousness of the spiritual life. If anything, it deepens it.

For love asks more of us than fear ever can.

Fear may control behavior for a time. But only love can truly transform desire.

Throughout this adaptation, I have tried to preserve Hildegard’s central vision: that creation is alive with divine meaning, that humanity matters deeply, that the soul participates in a cosmic drama of transformation, and that God continually calls creation toward harmony, beauty, mercy, and life.

The language has changed.

The imagery has softened.

But the longing beneath it remains the same.

Both Hildegard’s work and this re-visioning seek to glorify God and reveal divine love to humanity.

If these reflections help even one weary soul slow down, breathe more deeply, soften what has hardened, or turn gently again toward the Light, then this work has fulfilled its purpose.

May these pages become a quiet place for you.

May they help you listen more deeply to the movements within your own soul.

And may the Living Light that guided Hildegard continue to guide all who seek truth with sincerity, humility, and love.
