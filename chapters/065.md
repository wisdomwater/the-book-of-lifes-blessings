## Day 65 - The Dragon in the Heart

> *Anger does not always arrive roaring. Sometimes it hides in the shadows of the soul, waiting for a moment to consume what is good.*

Again I heard the voice of the Living Light telling me that what had been shown was true.

Those who are entangled in anger and hatred should not wait for a later purification if they can begin now. While they still breathe the air of this world, they should grieve what they have done, humble themselves, and accept the sharp medicine of repentance. Better to be wounded into life now than to remain untouched and fall into deeper torment later.

Those who let hatred grow into a second nature must resist it fiercely. Those whose anger rises without rooted hatred must still be corrected, though with a lighter remedy. For the soul must learn the weight of what it carries.

Then I was shown the nature of anger itself.

It is a grave defect, a dark force that lives as though in the serpent’s den. It waits for the mind to loosen its guard. It threatens intelligence. It drives a person outside himself.

I saw that anger gnashes its teeth like a thief. It steals virtue. It devours whatever good begins to grow. It is like a dragon burning all it touches, and like a bandit plundering whatever it can seize.

When anger takes hold, the wise become foolish. The patient lose their balance. The gentle are turned bitter. What was measured becomes excessive. What was upright leans toward ruin.

It rejects kindness. It resists the sweetness of God’s commands. It is a hard crag that tries to break justice apart. It is murder already stirring in the heart before the hand has moved.

And I understood with trembling: once anger possesses a person, it can make him forget both heaven and earth. He no longer sees clearly. He is scattered within himself.

Yet even this was shown in mercy.

For the Living Light does not uncover the wound in order to shame the soul, but to call it back before it is fully consumed.


#### What This Vision Means

This vision shows anger as more than a passing emotion. It is a spiritual force that can become habitual, then shaping, then destructive. Hildegard sees it as something parasitic: it does not create, but corrodes. It feeds on the good already present and tries to burn it down.

The imagery of dragon, thief, and bandit is important. Anger is not portrayed as strength. It feels powerful, but its true work is theft. It steals intelligence, patience, moderation, gentleness, and inner order. It makes a person forget who they are.

The distinction between anger with hatred and anger without hatred also matters. Not every surge of anger is the same. But all anger must be brought into the light. If left unexamined, even momentary anger can become a settled bitterness.

The deeper truth is that anger opposes harmony. It wants to shake what should remain ordered. It wants to scatter what God would gather. So the call is not only to suppress anger, but to let it be transformed before it becomes a home for violence.


#### Living This Vision

Most anger begins in hurt, fear, humiliation, or frustration. It can feel justified. Sometimes it even feels necessary, like a shield.

But this vision asks a harder question: what is your anger doing inside you?

Is it clarifying truth, or clouding it? Is it protecting life, or quietly devouring tenderness, patience, and reason?

You do not need to pretend you never feel angry. The task is to notice it early, before it becomes a second nature. Pause when heat rises. Delay speech. Step away if you need to. Bring the anger before God before you bring it to another person.

A small act of restraint may feel weak in the moment. Spiritually, it is strength.

Repentance here is not self-hatred. It is retraining the soul. It is choosing not to let anger make your inner home in you.

The dragon grows where it is fed.

But it weakens where it is named, resisted, and surrendered.
