## 95 - The Clear Turning

> *You do not lose God all at once. You return by the smallest turning.*

I saw a great cloud gathering before the soul—thick with movement, layered with shadow and light. It shifted constantly, never fully settling, drawing attention into its endless motion. Thoughts moved through it one after another, each carrying its own urgency, each offering another path to follow. Some promised clarity. Others stirred fear, planning, analysis, memory, or distraction. The cloud was alive with movement, and the soul found itself leaning toward it almost without noticing.

Step by step, it placed its weight upon what would not hold.

Not through rebellion.

Through habit.

The cloud offered constant movement but no firm ground beneath it. The more the soul followed its shifting patterns, the harder it became to feel where it truly stood. Everything seemed uncertain because everything depended upon thoughts that changed from moment to moment.

Then something quieter revealed itself.

Not above the cloud, but beneath it.

A stillness remained there—steady, unchanged by the restless swirling overhead. It did not argue with the thoughts or attempt to silence them by force. It simply endured beneath them all, calm and unmoved.

And when the soul paused, even briefly, its attention began to shift.

Not toward the many voices within the cloud, but toward the quiet presence beneath them.

Its footing changed.

The soul slowly withdrew its weight from the endless movement of thought and returned to what was steady. The cloud did not disappear immediately. The mind continued moving as minds do. Yet its hold loosened. The air began to clear just enough for the way forward to become visible again.

And I understood then that the soul is rarely lost all at once.

It drifts gradually through countless small inattentions.

And it returns through equally small acts of remembrance.

### Living the Vision

Much of human suffering unfolds through the mind’s endless movement. Thoughts multiply rapidly—plans, fears, questions, imagined futures, old conversations, self-judgments, uncertainties. Many of these thoughts seem important, even necessary. Yet when we unconsciously stand upon every thought as though it were solid ground, the soul becomes unstable and exhausted.

We begin living entirely inside the cloud.

The contemplative life offers another possibility—not the elimination of thought, but a different relationship to it. We learn that awareness itself is deeper than the constant stream passing through the mind. Beneath the noise there remains a quiet steadiness that does not shift every time a new fear or impulse appears.

Returning to this steadiness rarely happens dramatically.

Often it begins with something very small: noticing the breath, pausing before reacting, becoming aware of the body, whispering a simple prayer, returning attention gently to the present moment. These acts may seem insignificant, yet they loosen the soul’s dependence upon the restless movement of thought.

We do not need to solve every thought before peace becomes possible.

We simply stop standing entirely upon them.

And slowly, clarity returns. Not because the cloud vanishes forever, but because the soul remembers there is something more trustworthy beneath it. From this deeper ground, we can respond to life with greater gentleness, wisdom, and presence.

No matter how crowded the mind becomes, the way back remains near.

A single turning is enough to begin again.

### Breath Prayer

**Inhale:** Bring me back to You

**Exhale:** I rest in what is clear
