## 87 - The Rooted Presence

> *What abides grows deep. What rests becomes whole.*

I saw a single tree standing alone in an open field. Its branches did not stretch wildly toward every passing current of wind or light. They extended with quiet balance, held within a still and steady form that seemed entirely at peace with where it had been planted. Around it the air moved constantly—winds carrying distant sounds across the grasses, shifting directions without warning, stirring everything exposed upon the earth.

But the tree did not follow the movement around it.

It remained.

Its roots reached downward into hidden depths, drawing life from a source unseen by those passing at a distance. Above, its leaves turned gently toward the light—not straining to be noticed, not grasping for attention, but simply receiving what was already being given. There was no urgency within it. No restless need to appear elsewhere or become something other than what it already was.

And because it remained present where it stood, life quietly gathered around it.

Birds rested within its branches. Shade formed beneath its canopy. The earth near its roots seemed softer, more alive. Nothing about the tree announced itself loudly, yet its stillness created space for life to flourish. It was not scattered across many places at once. It was whole because it had learned how to dwell deeply where it was.

And I understood then that there is a form of presence the soul can only discover through remaining.

Not through endless movement.

Not through constant reaching.

But through abiding long enough for roots to deepen beneath the surface of ordinary life.

**Living the Vision**

Much within modern life encourages the soul to scatter itself. Attention is constantly pulled outward toward noise, urgency, productivity, comparison, and the fear of missing something important somewhere else. Even while standing in one place, the mind often lives in ten others at once. We begin to believe that significance depends upon constant movement and visibility.

And yet the heart quietly grows weary when it is never allowed to rest deeply where it already is.

The contemplative life invites another rhythm—one rooted not in withdrawal from the world, but in presence within it. We begin learning how to inhabit our own lives more fully instead of endlessly escaping them through distraction or restless striving. This kind of rootedness does not make us smaller. It allows us to become more whole.

There are depths within the soul that only reveal themselves through staying.

Through listening patiently. Through remaining with relationships, practices, places, griefs, joys, and ordinary moments long enough for hidden roots to form beneath them. Much of what is most meaningful in human life cannot be rushed into existence. Love deepens slowly. Wisdom deepens slowly. Healing deepens slowly.

And when we stop scattering ourselves across endless outward pursuits, something within us begins gathering again.

We feel more coherent.

More grounded.

More alive in the present moment instead of perpetually reaching beyond it.

Like the tree rooted quietly beneath an open sky, the soul discovers that it does not need to be everywhere in order to become full of life.

It only needs to remain deeply where it truly is.

**Breath Prayer**

**Inhale:** Root me here

**Exhale:** Let me be whole
