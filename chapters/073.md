## Day 73 — The Tongue of Wind

> *When truth is bent for gain, the soul loses its footing.*

I saw a figure hidden in a darkness so dense that no clear form could be seen.  
Only a distorted shape flickered within it—something human, yet misshapen.

It stood upon hardened black ground, like dry foam.  
At times, flames burst upward beneath it.

And it spoke:

“Who truly speaks honestly?  
If I were sincere, I would lose what I have.  
If another rises, I must fall.

So I will speak words like wind—  
words that bring me honor.

What I cannot gain one way, I will gain another.  
If I were always truthful, I would be left with nothing.”

It laughed at honesty, calling it weakness.  
It praised its own shifting voice—  
changing, bending, multiplying itself so no one could grasp it.

Then the voice from the storming cloud answered:

“O tongue of deception,  
you burn with insult and falsehood.

You feed on what is empty.  
You do not know where you are going.”

But another voice rose—clear and steady:

“I am Truth.

I stand as a pillar in the ways of God.  
I sound like a trumpet that does not falter.

All creation holds to me—  
the heavens, the earth, the waters beneath.

I reveal what is real,  
and I do not waver.”

And I saw:

Truth was radiant, adorned like one welcomed into a king’s court.  
Falsehood, though loud, was small—  
a worm in the dust, soon to be trampled.

#### What This Vision Means

This image reveals the subtle temptation to manipulate truth for advantage.

Falsehood rarely appears as outright lies alone. It often comes as exaggeration, omission, or strategic speech—words shaped to gain approval, avoid loss, or control perception.

The figure’s reasoning is familiar: “If I tell the truth, I will lose.” This fear drives the distortion.

But the vision shows the deeper cost.

Falsehood destabilizes the soul. It creates fragmentation—one voice for one moment, another for the next. Over time, a person loses clarity, even within themselves.

Truth, by contrast, is steady. It aligns a person with reality—both inwardly and outwardly. It may not always bring immediate gain, but it establishes something lasting.

The imagery of creation itself being truthful suggests that truth is woven into the structure of existence. To live truthfully is to live in harmony with what is.

#### Living This Vision

Notice when you feel the urge to adjust your words.

To impress.  
To avoid conflict.  
To protect your image.

These moments are invitations.

You do not need to become harsh or blunt.  
But you can become honest—gently, clearly, and without manipulation.

Ask yourself:

Am I speaking to reveal,  
or to control?

Truth may feel risky.  
But it brings a quiet stability that nothing else can.

Even small acts of honesty matter.

They anchor you.

And over time, your life becomes simpler—  
less tangled, less divided.

Like a clear sound that does not waver.
