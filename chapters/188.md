## Day 188 - The Barren Place

> *Worldly sorrow dries the heart when trust disappears. But even in desolation, God asks the soul to remember joy.*

After this, I saw other evil spirits in the crowd.

They cried out:

“What kind of God would allow such hatred?”

These spirits dragged human beings into sadness over life in the world. They persuaded them to wither inwardly, to grieve their own existence, and to sink into despair.

Then I saw a barren place.

It had no water.

It was full of worms and surrounded by darkness.

There the souls were punished who, while living in the body, had been overcome by the sadness of life in the world.

Evil spirits drove them back and forth through that place with whips of fire, saying:

“Why did you not put your trust in your God?”

They were in that barren place because they had not lived in the joy of heavenly things. Instead, despairing over earthly imperfections, they had fallen into worldly sadness.

They suffered worms because they had lived in bitterness of heart.

They suffered darkness because they had neglected the holiness that does not fade.

And they were afflicted by evil spirits because, tangled in these sorrows, they had not trusted God.

And the living Spirit saw and understood these things.

#### What This Vision Means

This vision speaks of a sorrow that becomes despair.

It is not ordinary sadness, grief, or lament. Those can be holy and healing. This is the sorrow that cuts the soul off from trust. It says life is only misery. It forgets heavenly joy and sees only earthly imperfection.

The barren place shows a heart without refreshment. No water flows there. Darkness surrounds it. Worms reveal the bitterness that grows when sorrow has no opening toward God.

The evil spirits’ question is piercing: “Why did you not trust?”

The vision does not shame pain. It exposes despair as a false guide.

#### Living This Vision

There are days when life feels barren.

Nothing seems to grow. Prayer feels dry. Hope feels far away.

This vision invites one small act of trust.

Not forced cheerfulness. Not denial. Just one turning of the heart toward God.

A breath. A tear. A whispered prayer. A remembered mercy.

Worldly sorrow says, “Nothing good remains.”

Faith says, “Even here, God has not left me.”
