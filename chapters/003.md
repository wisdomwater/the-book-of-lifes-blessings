## Day 3 — The Flowers of the World

> *I saw how the soul can cling to beauty that fades,
> and how quickly the garden of illusion withers.*

In the vision I saw the first image appear.

It had the form of a man, dark in color like the blackness of Ethiopia. He stood naked, with no covering upon him. His arms and legs wrapped themselves tightly around a great tree.

The tree was enormous. Its branches stretched outward in many directions, and upon them bloomed countless flowers—bright and varied, beautiful to look upon.

The man eagerly plucked these flowers.

He gathered them in his hands and spoke proudly, saying:

“Mine are all the kingdoms of the world with their flowers and ornaments. Why should I wither when I hold such vitality in my hands? Why should I grow old when I can remain in the freshness of youth? Why should my beautiful sight turn into blindness?

If that were to happen, I would be ashamed.

So I will enjoy the beauty of this world for as long as I can. I will embrace it with delight.

I do not know what this other life is that people speak of. These stories I hear mean nothing to me.”

But scarcely had he finished speaking when the great tree began to wither.

Its leaves shriveled. Its flowers faded. Its strength drained away until the tree dried from its branches down to its very roots.

Then it collapsed into the darkness I had seen before.

And the figure who clung to it fell with it into that same darkness.

Then I heard a voice from the stormy cloud answer him.

“You commit the greatest foolishness if you believe you can possess true life in a spark of ash.

You do not seek the life where beauty never fades and youth never withers. You have no true light. A dark fog surrounds you, and you hide within the desires of human will like a worm hidden in the earth.

You live only for a moment.

Like hay in the field, you dry up and fall into destruction together with the things you call your flowers.

But I am a pillar of heavenly harmony.

I give the joy of the true life.

I do not destroy life. Instead, I break what wounds you. I am the mirror of the virtues, where every faithful soul may see itself clearly.

But you run along the paths of darkness, and the works of your hands produce only rebellion.”

#### What This Vision Means

The figure clinging to the flowering tree represents the human soul captured by worldly attachment.

The flowers symbolize beauty, pleasure, power, and the many delights of earthly life. None of these things are evil in themselves. Yet when the soul clings to them as if they were eternal, illusion begins to grow.

The man believes he possesses vitality and youth simply because he holds the flowers in his hands. He believes that enjoyment can protect him from the reality of aging, loss, and death.

But the vision reveals the truth: the tree itself is temporary.

The moment he declares his confidence in worldly beauty, the tree withers from its roots. What seemed strong and flourishing is suddenly revealed to be fragile.

Divine Love answers him from the stormy cloud. The voice reminds us that earthly pleasures are like sparks of ash—brief flashes that cannot sustain life forever.

True life is found in a different place: in the eternal harmony of divine love, where beauty does not fade and youth does not decay.

The vision is not a condemnation of beauty.

It is a warning against mistaking passing beauty for eternal life.

#### Living This Vision

Every generation is tempted to believe that fulfillment can be found in the flowers of the world.

We reach for success, admiration, comfort, pleasure, and security. These things are not meaningless—but they cannot hold the weight of our deepest longing.

Like the figure in the vision, we sometimes cling tightly to what is fading.

We tell ourselves that youth will last longer than it does. That achievement will satisfy the soul. That more beauty, more pleasure, more control will finally quiet the restless heart.

But eventually the flowers begin to fall.

The spiritual life invites us to enjoy the beauty of the world without clinging to it.

When our roots sink deeper into divine love, we discover a different kind of vitality—one that does not dry up with time.

The flowers of the world may fade.

But the garden of the soul can still grow.
