## Day 52 – The Laughter After Wrath

> *I saw a joy that rose from anger, but it was hollow and turned downward into darkness.*

After the fire of anger had passed, another figure emerged—one that seemed, at first, lighter.

It bore the shape of a man from head to waist, yet its hands were like those of a monkey—restless, imitative, unsteady. Below, its body bent and crouched like a goat, always leaning downward, never rising.

This figure laughed.

But its laughter was not clean.

It rejoiced in the suffering of others. It delighted in humiliation. It took pleasure not in goodness, but in the fall of another. Its joy was sharpened by revenge, as though anger had simply changed its face.

Its feet sank into darkness.

Step by step, it moved without seeing, without awareness, without memory of what is holy. The light above it did not call to it, for it had already turned away.

It wore no garment.

It stood exposed, stripped of true joy, clothed only in impulse and desire. It reached eagerly for passing pleasures, grasping what was fleeting, forgetting what was eternal.

And I saw that this joy did not lift the soul.

It lowered it.

#### What This Vision Means

Foolish joy is the counterfeit of true delight.

It arises not from love, but from distortion—often following anger, as a kind of release or vindication. Instead of healing, it deepens the wound.

The mixed form of the figure reveals a divided nature. The human mind, made for discernment, is pulled downward by instinct and impulse. The monkey-like hands suggest imitation without understanding. The goat-like stance shows a life bent toward lower desires.

Its nakedness is especially revealing. Without spiritual joy—the joy rooted in God—the soul stands uncovered, lacking depth, stability, and direction.

This joy forgets eternity.

It lives only for the moment, often at the expense of truth, compassion, and dignity.

#### Living This Vision

There is a kind of satisfaction that comes when someone who hurt you suffers.

It can feel justified.  
It can feel like balance restored.

But this vision gently asks:

What is this doing to your heart?

Does it make you more whole?  
More peaceful?  
More aligned with who you truly are?

Or does it pull you downward?

True joy does not require another’s pain. It does not depend on comparison or revenge. It is quiet, steady, and deeply rooted.

The invitation here is simple, but not easy:

Let go of the need to rejoice in another’s fall.

Turn your desire toward what endures.

Because the joy you seek cannot be found in darkness.
