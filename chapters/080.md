## 80 - The Quiet Path of Yes

> *You do not lose yourself in surrender. You come home to what is true.*

I saw a figure moving quickly across an open landscape, turning again and again as though uncertain where to go. Each step seemed to lead into another direction, and the ground beneath the figure felt unsettled, like dust stirred constantly by restless wind. There was motion everywhere, yet no true arrival. The movement carried an urgency within it, as though the figure feared what might happen if it ever became still long enough to listen.

And not far away, there was a path.

It was simple and clear beneath the light—quiet, steady, unadorned. It did not demand attention or attempt to persuade. It simply remained where it was, patient and unchanged. Beside the path stood another presence, calm and unhurried. There was no force within it, no pressure or insistence. Only a gentle readiness, as though it understood that the soul cannot be driven into truth before it is willing to see.

For a long while, the figure continued wandering.

Then, almost imperceptibly, it paused.

And in that small stillness, something within began to soften. The endless turning slowed. The noise of reaction quieted enough for another awareness to emerge. The path had not suddenly appeared. It had been there all along. But now the figure had become open enough to recognize it.

And I saw that surrender is often much quieter than we imagine. It is not the loss of self, but the releasing of what keeps the self scattered. It is the gentle willingness to stop fleeing long enough to discover the ground already waiting beneath us.

**Living the Vision**

There are moments in life when something within us quietly resists what we already know to be true. The resistance may not appear dramatic. Often it comes clothed in reasonable explanations, endless analysis, distraction, or the constant search for another option that feels safer or more comfortable. The mind keeps moving so that the deeper invitation of the heart does not have to be fully faced.

Yet beneath this movement, many people sense a subtle unrest.

The soul grows weary when it continually turns away from its own deepest knowing. We may remain busy, productive, even outwardly successful, while inwardly feeling fragmented and uncertain. Part of us longs for clarity, but another part fears what surrender might require.

The contemplative life reveals that surrender is rarely about force. It begins with a small willingness—a quiet yes spoken inwardly before the whole path is visible. This yes does not demand perfect certainty. It simply opens the heart enough to take one honest step toward what brings life, truth, and peace.

And over time, this willingness creates coherence within us. Our inner and outer lives begin to move in the same direction. We become less divided against ourselves. Not because every question has been answered, but because we are no longer running from the light we have already been given.

The path often appears slowly.

But each sincere yes helps us recognize it more clearly.
