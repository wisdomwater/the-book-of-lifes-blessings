## Day 148 - The Shape of Injustice

> *Injustice leaps without wisdom and roots itself in mud. It wants its own will, but cannot stand before truth.*

Then I saw again the first image among the vices.

It was the form of Injustice.

There was no joy in it. It carried the memory of the first rebellion, that ancient turning away from what is good and ordered. Wherever it moved, it sought to undo what was fair.

Its head was like a fawn—quick, restless, leaping without wisdom. So too the unjust mind throws itself forward, trampling good counsel and clear understanding. It rushes toward the cliff, thinking itself clever, always seeking what seems useful for itself.

Its tail was like a bear, heavy with instability and roaring malice. It resisted everything. It fought everything. Yet when true judgment came, it was brought down and reduced to nothing.

The rest of its body was like a pig, sunk in mud.

For those who give themselves to injustice become soiled by it. They lie down in its dirt. Their works twist and coil through injury, violence, and harm. They do not seek upright wisdom. They do not listen to the counsel of justice.

They want all things to bend toward themselves.

They want their will to stand above others.

They want the world arranged around their appetite.

But Justice answered this vice.

Justice spoke not with cruelty, but with firmness. It warned the human soul not to imitate this creature, not to enter its mud, not to mistake self-will for strength.

For injustice may leap, roar, and trample.

But it cannot endure the clear gaze of what is true.


#### What This Vision Means

Injustice is shown as disordered movement.

The fawn-like head suggests quickness without depth. The bear-like tail suggests brute resistance. The pig-like body reveals the final condition of injustice: it becomes comfortable in what is unclean.

This is not merely social injustice, though it includes that. It is also the inner habit of bending reality around the self.

Injustice says, “My will must prevail.”

It cannot receive correction because correction feels like defeat. It cannot honor others because others feel like obstacles.

The imagery reveals how injustice corrupts the whole person—thought, movement, desire, and action.

Yet Justice stands as a living presence. Justice does not merely punish. Justice restores proportion. It teaches the soul to stand upright again.

To become just is to stop trampling.

It is to let truth order our strength.


#### Living This Vision

Injustice begins subtly.

It appears when we excuse what we would condemn in someone else. It appears when we bend the truth to protect our image. It appears when we demand grace for ourselves while withholding it from others.

The invitation is not shame.

The invitation is honesty.

Where have I wanted my will more than what is right?
Where have I used strength without tenderness?
Where have I ignored the harm my choices create?

Justice does not ask us to hate ourselves.

It asks us to become whole.

To step out of the mud.
To stop rushing toward the cliff.
To let truth steady our feet.

The soul becomes free when it no longer needs to dominate.
