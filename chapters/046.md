## Day 46 – The Tree of Worldly Love

> *I saw a dark figure clinging to a flowering tree, grasping beauty that was already fading.*

Within the fog of unbelief, I beheld many forms of vice, each shifting and multiplying, as though one darkness gave birth to another. Among them stood a figure shaped like a man, yet blackened and dim, as if no light had ever touched him.

He was naked, stripped of all holiness, and wrapped himself tightly around a great tree bursting with flowers. The branches spread wide, heavy with blossoms of every kind—beautiful, alluring, and countless.

He gathered these flowers into his hands, clutching them eagerly. Each one seemed to promise delight. Each one whispered of pleasure, possession, and praise. And as he held them, he seemed satisfied, drawing them toward himself as though they could fill something empty within him.

But I saw the root of the tree.

It was dry.

And slowly, almost without notice, the tree began to fall. Its beauty could not sustain it. Its abundance could not save it. And when it collapsed, the figure clinging to it fell with it—down into the same darkness from which it had come.

Yet even as he fell, he did not seem to know it.

So tightly was he bound to what was passing that he could not see what was eternal.

#### What This Vision Means

Worldly love is not simply the enjoyment of created things. It is the binding of the soul to them as if they were life itself.

The flowers are not evil in themselves—they represent the many delights, achievements, and desires that fill human life. But when the soul clings to them as its source of meaning, it becomes entangled in something that cannot endure.

The dryness at the root reveals the truth: what is not grounded in divine life cannot sustain the soul. It may bloom for a time, even abundantly, but its end is already written within it.

This vision also reveals a deeper blindness. The one who clings to vanity does not perceive their fall. They remain convinced of their stability, even as everything beneath them gives way.

Unbelief is not merely doubt—it is disconnection from the source of life. And from that disconnection, every other distortion grows.

#### Living This Vision

This vision invites a quiet and honest question:

What am I clinging to?

There are things in your life that feel beautiful, meaningful, even necessary. And many of them are good. But when they become the place you draw your identity, your worth, or your security, they begin to take the shape of that tree.

You may not notice the root drying.

You may not feel the falling at first.

But there is a gentle invitation here—not to reject the world, but to loosen your grip. To enjoy what is given without binding your soul to it.

To let your roots go deeper than what can fade.

Because only what is rooted in God can hold you when everything else falls.
