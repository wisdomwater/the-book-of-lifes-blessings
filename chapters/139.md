## Day 139 - The Turning Wheel

> *Inconstancy spins around itself, always moving, yet never arriving.*

Then I saw another image in the darkness.

There was a wheel, like a millstone, turning as if driven by violent winds. Its motion was restless and circular. Upon its spokes were fixed four sticks, each as long as the height of a standing human being.

Within the spokes stood a human figure.

It grasped one stick in each hand, while the other two stood behind its back. As the wheel turned, the figure turned with it.

Its hair was black and curly. Its hands were like the forelegs of a monkey. Its feet were like the feet of hawks. Its garment was marked with black and white bands.

At times, the figure spread out a net to catch animals.

But it caught nothing.

Then it spoke.

“Why should I ignore what I am? I do what I know. If I did not act this way, I would be foolish.

“Many people behave in ways that amaze me. The wise become fools. The poor become rich. The despised receive honor.

“As for me, I say what I am. I show what I want. I do not abandon what I have. I do what I can.

“While I am able, I will not be empty.

“A craftsman who refuses to practice the work he knows would be useless. He would neglect his art.

“Fortune herself teaches this: when a person has prosperity, he does what he wants. But when prosperity ends, he can no longer do what he desires.

“And this is honesty.”

The wheel kept turning.

The net opened and closed upon empty air.

The figure moved, but did not stand. It reached, but did not receive. It spoke of honesty, yet seemed bound to motion without direction.

And I watched this restless turning in the darkness.

#### What This Vision Means

This image reveals the instability of inconstancy.

The wheel turns, but does not travel. The figure is busy, but not fruitful. The net is spread, but nothing is caught. This is motion without rootedness.

Inconstancy often sounds practical. It says, “I am only doing what I can. I am only using the opportunity before me. Fortune changes, so I must take what I can while I can.”

There is a kind of truth in this, but it is not deep truth. The vice speaks from survival, impulse, and shifting circumstance. It confuses movement with purpose.

The black and white bands suggest division. The monkey-like hands grasp. The hawk-like feet seize. The whole figure is built for restless taking, not faithful abiding.

Because the passage ends before the answering virtue speaks, the vision remains suspended. We are left inside the turning itself, feeling the exhaustion of a soul that cannot become still.

Sometimes the absence of an answer is part of the teaching.

#### Living This Vision

Many of us know the turning wheel.

We move from task to task, desire to desire, fear to fear. We call it productivity. We call it adaptability. Sometimes it is necessary. But sometimes we are simply spinning.

This vision asks us to notice what our motion is producing.

Are we becoming more whole?  
More loving?  
More grounded?  
More truthful?

Or are we only reaching, grasping, and reacting?

Inconstancy is not healed by force. It is healed by returning to a center.

One faithful practice.  
One grounded commitment.  
One place where the soul says, “Here I will remain.”

Even before the virtue answers, the wheel itself teaches us: movement without rootedness becomes captivity.
