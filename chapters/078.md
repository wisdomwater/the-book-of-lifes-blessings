## 78 - The Dew of Living Desire

> *Desire is not the enemy. It is the garden waiting to be tended.*

I saw a figure lying in stillness upon the earth. At first it seemed peaceful, yet the ground beneath it felt dry and weary, as though it had long carried a hidden thirst. Around the figure grew tender things—petals opening toward light, leaves unfolding softly in the air, fragile signs of life reaching outward. And the figure touched them gently, almost lovingly. Yet wherever its hands lingered too long, the greenness slowly faded.

The withering did not happen violently. It came quietly, like water disappearing from shallow soil beneath a relentless sun. Something essential was being drawn away—not because the figure desired life too deeply, but because its longing had forgotten how to receive without consuming.

Then another presence began to gather.

It arrived the way morning dew settles upon a field before dawn fully breaks—a coolness in the air, a softness that asked nothing for itself. Clear drops formed upon every surface, resting lightly upon petals, leaves, and open ground. The dew did not grasp or demand. It simply remained close to what it touched.

And where it rested, life returned.

The earth softened again. The flowers slowly opened. What had become strained beneath restless hunger began to breathe once more. And I saw that this gentleness did not destroy desire—it healed it. Desire itself was not the wound. The wound came from forgetting that love cannot flourish where everything is taken before it can fully live.

True desire moves differently. It receives and gives in the same breath. It nourishes what it touches. It remains connected to wonder rather than possession.

**Living the Vision**

Many people carry a quiet fear of their own desires. They have seen how longing can become compulsive, how craving can hollow out the heart, how the pursuit of pleasure or affirmation can leave behind exhaustion instead of peace. And so they begin to believe that desire itself must be dangerous.

But the deeper spiritual life does not ask us to become numb.

Desire is part of what makes us human. It draws us toward beauty, intimacy, creativity, tenderness, truth, and communion. Without desire, the soul would never move toward love at all. Yet desire becomes distorted when it is driven by emptiness alone—when we grasp at experiences, people, or comforts in hopes they will finally quiet the ache within us.

The contemplative path invites a gentler relationship with longing. Instead of reacting immediately to every impulse, we learn to remain present with what we feel. We allow desire to breathe. We listen beneath its urgency. Often, beneath compulsive wanting lies a deeper hunger—for connection, rest, belonging, or tenderness.

And when desire is held with patience rather than fear, it begins to change. What once felt consuming slowly becomes life-giving. The heart learns how to love without devouring, how to receive without clinging, how to delight without losing itself.

Like dew resting softly upon a garden, healed desire nourishes life wherever it touches.

**Breath Prayer**

**Inhale:** Let my desire be gentle

**Exhale:** Restore what is living
