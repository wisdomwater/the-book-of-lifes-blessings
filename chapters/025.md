## 25 - The Heart That Softens

> *Even what feels closed can open. Mercy falls like quiet dew.*

A dim stillness lingered at the far edge of the field, quiet and withdrawn beneath a pale and colorless sky. Nothing there felt openly hostile or broken. It simply seemed distant, as though some part of it had slowly forgotten how to move toward life again. The air itself carried a feeling of separation, gentle but unmistakable.

Within the stillness hovered a shape only faintly formed, like mist gathered without direction across cold ground before dawn. It did not reach outward. It did not turn toward what surrounded it. It simply remained apart—watching from a distance without entering into the movement of life around it.

The space surrounding it felt untouched, almost suspended. Nothing had passed between this presence and the world for a very long time. No exchange. No offering. No receiving. Only a quiet inwardness that had hardened slowly into isolation.

Then, without warning and without sound, something soft entered the field.

It did not descend dramatically from above or arrive with force. It appeared more like dew forming silently in the early morning—gentle moisture settling over stone, leaf, and soil alike without preference or demand. Nothing in the field was required to earn it. Nothing was pressured to respond.

And yet, the earth received it.

Slowly, almost imperceptibly, what had once been dry began to loosen. What had remained still for so long softened beneath the quiet touch of mercy. The mist itself did not disappear entirely, but something within it began to change. A subtle awareness awakened—a recognition of the life it had stood apart from for so long.

And within that awakening, the smallest opening appeared.

Not a dramatic decision.

Not sudden transformation.

Only a quiet yielding to what had always been present and gently offered all along.

### Living the Vision

There are seasons when withdrawing inward can feel safer than remaining open. After disappointment, exhaustion, grief, or overwhelm, the heart sometimes pulls back quietly from life—not out of cruelty, but out of weariness. We become more guarded. More distant. Less willing to risk tenderness again.

At first, this distance can even feel protective. Necessary. A way of conserving what little energy remains.

Yet over time, many people begin sensing another kind of ache beneath the numbness. Not dramatic suffering, but a dimming. A subtle loss of aliveness. The soul was not created merely to survive behind closed walls. It longs for connection, presence, giving, receiving, and participation in something larger than itself.

The healing of the heart rarely begins through force. We do not shame ourselves back into openness. Nor do we suddenly become vulnerable through sheer willpower alone. More often, restoration arrives quietly, through small moments that soften us again.

A kind word received unexpectedly. A moment of honest listening. A simple act of compassion. A realization that we do not need to carry everything alone. These movements may seem small, yet they resemble dew upon dry ground—gentle enough not to overwhelm, steady enough to nourish what has grown weary.

Mercy works this way.

It does not force the heart open violently. It arrives patiently, again and again, until something within us remembers how to receive life once more. And even the smallest yielding matters. One softened breath. One honest prayer. One moment of allowing ourselves to feel again.

You do not need to become someone entirely different in order to heal. Often the soul simply needs permission to soften toward the love that has already been quietly flowing toward it all along.

### Breath Prayer

**Inhale:** Soften my heart

**Exhale:** Let mercy flow
