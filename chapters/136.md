## Day 136 - The Crown of Justice

> *Justice sees the dignity of every creature. It does not despise what God has made.*

I saw the Man turn toward the South and the West.

The earth beneath him was alive from his knees downward. It held moisture, strength, seed, and the greening beauty of life. It seemed adorned by the vigor of the Man himself.

For the earth brings forth living things. All earthly creatures are shaped from earth. The earth is the material through which God works in humanity, and it is also the matter taken up in the humanity of the Son of God.

Then, within the mist that held many vices, I saw a strange image appear.

Its head was like a young deer. Its tail was like a bear. The rest of its body was like a pig.

And it spoke with arrogance.

“Upon whom should I pour out justice? Upon no one. Why should I concern myself with another? I am wiser than others. I know the sun, the moon, the stars, and all created things. Why should I pretend not to know? What I have is better and more useful than what others have. I am equal to those who judge and discern all things.”

Then a voice answered from the storm-cloud stretched across the sky.

“What are you saying, shameless and diabolical art? God has ordered all things to be considered with care. The more one person learns from another, the more knowledge grows.

“God gave humanity the science of the eyes, so that danger may be seen and avoided. If a person does not care for what has been entrusted to them, what creature will obey or serve?

“Humanity works with creation. The garden is dug with the hoe. The field is turned with the plow. Oxen labor beside human hands. Each creature is used according to its purpose.

“Why do you despise the human being, in whom heaven and earth are understood?

“I recognize the gifts of the Holy Spirit in humanity. I honor the work of God. I wear the royal diadem among creatures, and I care for them with reverence.

“I have flowed from the living source. No earthly thing can frighten me. I rose with the dawn. I am beloved of God, and with God I remain.

“I am the sap of trees that do not dry in winter. I dwell on Mount Zion. I walk in the meekness of the Lamb. I rise in his victory.

“I am the victory of the King, and I will not be defeated.”

#### What This Vision Means

Injustice speaks as self-sufficiency. It sees no need to care for anyone. It believes its own knowledge is enough. It looks upon others and says, “Why should I be responsible for them?”

But Justice answers with relationship.

In Hildegard’s vision, creation is not isolated. The earth, animals, tools, human labor, wisdom, and divine gifts all belong together. Nothing exists for itself alone. Justice is the right ordering of care.

The strange body of Injustice reveals distortion. A deer’s head, a bear’s tail, and a pig’s body suggest instincts pulled apart from harmony. Injustice is not whole. It uses perception, strength, and appetite without reverence.

Justice, by contrast, is rooted in God. It is sap, dawn, diadem, temple, altar, and victory. It does not dominate creation; it serves within it.

Justice is not cold judgment. It is faithful care for what God has made.

#### Living This Vision

Injustice often begins quietly.

It begins when we stop seeing others as entrusted to us. When we say, “That is not my concern.” When our knowledge makes us proud instead of tender. When we use people, creatures, or the earth without reverence.

Justice invites us to look again.

Who has God placed near you?  
What has been entrusted to your care?  
Where are you tempted to despise what feels weaker, slower, or less useful?

To live this vision is to practice honorable attention.

Justice does not require us to fix everything. But it does ask us not to turn away from love. It asks us to become steady, rooted, and awake.

Like sap hidden in the tree, justice may seem quiet. But it keeps life flowing.
