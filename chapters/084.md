## 84 - The Quiet Song Within

> *You do not need to feel to begin. A small turning becomes a song.*

I saw a figure standing at the edge of a shifting cloud. Darkness gathered in some places while light moved faintly through others, and nothing around it seemed fully still. The figure kept its gaze fixed outward, searching only for what could be touched, managed, or understood. Its attention moved constantly across the changing world, yet something in the atmosphere felt unfinished—as though a deeper harmony lingered just beyond hearing.

There was movement everywhere, but no music.

Then, very softly, a sound began to rise.

It did not come from outside the figure, nor did it break suddenly into the air. It emerged quietly from within the silence itself, like the gentle strings of a zither being touched with patient care. The melody did not compete with the noise of the world. It did not force itself forward or demand attention. It simply continued—steady, clear, and almost hidden beneath everything else.

And as the sound endured, something around the figure began to change.

The cloud slowly thinned. The restless shifting eased. For the first time, the figure paused long enough to truly listen. And within that listening, something awakened—not through dramatic revelation, but through remembrance. The music did not explain the world. It aligned the soul with something deeper than confusion.

And I saw that this quiet song had always been present.

Waiting patiently beneath distraction, beneath fear, beneath the endless outward turning of the mind.

Waiting to be heard again.

### Living the Vision

There are seasons when the soul becomes almost entirely outward-facing. We focus on responsibilities, problems, schedules, opinions, distractions, and the endless stream of things demanding our attention. Life becomes something to manage rather than inhabit. And when we no longer feel connected to anything deeper, it is easy to assume that the deeper presence itself has disappeared.

Yet absence of feeling is not absence of reality.

The contemplative life often begins very quietly—not with certainty or powerful emotion, but with a simple willingness to turn inward for a moment. A brief pause before reacting. A breath offered in silence. A small prayer spoken without knowing whether anything is listening. These movements may seem insignificant at first, especially when the heart feels numb or distracted.

But the soul remembers through repetition.

Over time, something subtle begins to shift. The nervous urgency of life loosens slightly. Inner space returns. We begin to notice moments of stillness that once passed unseen. A sense of presence quietly emerges—not dramatic, not overwhelming, but steady enough to accompany us through ordinary life.

Prayer, then, becomes less about achieving spiritual feelings and more about learning how to listen again.

The deeper music was never absent.

We simply became too hurried to hear it.

And every gentle return helps the heart remember the song already waiting within it.
