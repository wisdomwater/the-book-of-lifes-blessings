## 6 - The Boundary of Knowing

> *There is a place where striving quiets, and you are held without needing to understand.*

I saw a vast expanse opening in every direction—wide beyond measure, spacious beyond the reach of thought. Above, light stretched endlessly upward until sight itself could follow no further. Below rested a deep and quiet stillness, like hidden waters untouched by storm or disturbance. And between these mysteries, the world turned gently within a balance so natural it required no force to sustain it.

Nothing within this great unfolding seemed anxious to define itself. Nothing strained beyond its place or grasped toward what did not belong to it. All things rested within a deeper harmony, as though creation itself trusted the wisdom carrying it. The trees did not demand to know the full shape of the forest. The rivers did not fear where their waters would lead. Everything belonged without needing to possess the whole.

Then I became aware of a soft boundary within the vision—not a wall, not a punishment, not an exclusion, but something more like a horizon at dusk. A place where movement naturally slowed. Where thought became quiet. Where the restless need to go further or know more gently loosened its hold. Beyond this boundary, nothing felt absent or forbidden. Instead, the unknown rested peacefully within a fullness too vast to be exhausted by understanding.

And I understood then that what could not yet be reached was not being withheld in cruelty. The mystery remained open, alive, and deeply present. Some things were simply held within a wholeness larger than the mind could carry all at once. The soul did not need to conquer this mystery in order to belong within it. It only needed to rest.

There, at the boundary of knowing, striving slowly dissolved into trust. And within that trust, a quiet peace began to emerge—not the peace of certainty, but the peace of being gently held inside something wiser than fear.

**Living the Vision**

The human mind naturally reaches outward. It wants clarity about the future, understanding for suffering, answers for uncertainty, and reassurance that life is unfolding correctly. There is tenderness in this longing; it comes from our desire to feel safe within a world that often feels fragile and unpredictable. Yet the soul can become exhausted when it believes it must solve every mystery before it is allowed to rest.

There are moments when our questions remain unanswered despite all our effort. We search, analyze, rehearse possibilities, and still the future stays hidden. At first this can feel deeply uncomfortable, even frightening. But eventually a quieter invitation begins to appear beneath the striving—the invitation not to abandon wisdom, but to soften at the edge of what cannot yet be known.

Spiritual maturity often involves learning that limits are not always punishments. Some boundaries protect the soul from carrying burdens it was never meant to hold alone. We are not required to possess complete understanding in order to live meaningful, faithful, compassionate lives. There is grace in allowing ourselves to remain human—finite, unfinished, still learning how to trust.

And perhaps this is one of the hidden mercies of the spiritual life: that we can stop reaching endlessly beyond ourselves and instead become fully present to what has already been given. The breath before us. The person beside us. The small light available for today. When we rest within these simple realities, peace slowly settles into the heart—not because every question has disappeared, but because we begin to trust the Presence holding even what we cannot yet understand.
