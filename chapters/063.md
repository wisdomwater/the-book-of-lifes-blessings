## Day 63 — The Lake of Hatred

> *What is fed in anger sinks into corruption; what is released returns to peace.*

I saw a mass of burning air, rushing like a violent wind, heated with a fierce and restless fire. Beneath it lay a vast black lake, thick with decay and foulness, alive with corruption.

Within that rotting depth, worms stirred—each with a single eye, writhing through the filth, their movements slow and terrible.

And the souls within it suffered.

These were the ones who had clung to anger and hatred while they lived. Not a passing flare, but something held tightly, guarded, and fed over time.

They were immersed in the decay.

The filth surrounded them.

The worms moved through them.

And above them, the burning air scorched them without relief.

I understood: the filth was their anger, turned inward and made visible. The worms were their hatred, alive and unyielding. The fire above was the fury they had carried, now pressing down upon them.

But I saw also others, moving near the edges of this place.

They had known anger—but had not held onto it. They had not rooted themselves in hatred. They saw the torment of the lake and trembled, but they were not drawn into it.

They passed by.

And I knew: anger itself is not the end. It is what we do with it that shapes us.

The Living Light revealed this silently.

That what is released does not bind.

But what is held becomes a world.


#### What This Vision Means

This vision distinguishes between anger and hatred.

Anger can arise quickly. It is often a response to pain, injustice, or fear. But hatred is something deeper—it is anger that has been kept, nurtured, and allowed to define the heart.

The lake represents this accumulation. What begins as a feeling becomes an environment. The soul begins to dwell in what it refuses to release.

The worms, with their singular gaze, suggest fixation—a mind that circles the same wounds again and again. The burning air above reflects the intensity of unrelenting fury.

Yet there is mercy here.

Those who do not cling to anger—who feel it but do not become it—are not bound by it. They are able to move past its pull.

The vision invites discernment, not suppression.

To feel is human.

To hold without releasing is what binds.


#### Living This Vision

You will feel anger.

It will rise, sometimes unexpectedly.

The question is not whether it comes—but whether it stays.

Notice what happens when you hold onto it. How it begins to shape your thoughts, your tone, your view of others.

It can become a quiet companion.

Or a hidden weight.

This vision invites you to practice release.

Not by pretending the anger isn’t there, but by allowing it to move through you without becoming your identity.

You might name it. Breathe through it. Offer it to God honestly, without shame.

Over time, something shifts.

The anger loosens.

The heart softens.

And what could have become a prison becomes a passage instead.
