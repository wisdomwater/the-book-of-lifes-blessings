## Day 137 - Strength in the Ashes

> *The soul that avoids all struggle becomes less alive. Strength is born through faithful endurance.*

Then I saw a second image.

It had the face of a child, yet its hair was white. It wore a pale-colored garment. Beneath that garment it withdrew its arms and hands. It covered its feet and all its members, so that I could not clearly see its form.

And it spoke in complaint.

“Why should I endure a narrow and laborious life? Why should I face so many troubles, since I have not committed many sins?

“Many people weep and complain. They wear down their bodies until they can barely live, and still they behave badly. They heap sin upon sin.

“What good are all these efforts?

“I avoid fatigue. I flee from labor. I keep away from what harms me, and by doing so I gain a better life than others. I want no work.

“If I flee from labor, will God condemn me?”

Then a voice answered from the storm-cloud.

“O ash of ashes. O spark of miserable decay. You have made yourself poisonous against the life of the human being, who is the image of God. Your works are useless.

“You cannot compare yourself even to worms who labor in their caves to find food, nor to birds who build nests and seek what they need to sustain their bodies.

“What living creature passes through this life without care?

“None.

“This life is far from the life of paradise, where the eyes lived in happiness and never grew dim. But you, unhappy one, live without the wisdom of God. You desire what no one can give you. You want to receive without effort what your numb laziness cannot obtain.

“But I serve in the royal chamber with the strength of the lion, in the humanity of the Savior. I sigh for all the good things of God. I fly everywhere like one extending a cloak.

“All peoples who desire to remain in the good call upon me. They long to have me.

“But you are regarded as a useless corpse.”

#### What This Vision Means

This vice appears childish and old at once. It has the immaturity of avoidance and the weariness of a life that has refused to grow. Its hidden hands and feet reveal a soul unwilling to act, unwilling to walk, unwilling to serve.

It calls labor unnecessary. It mistakes comfort for wisdom. It wants a life without effort, without sorrow, without the demands of love.

The virtue answers as Fortitude.

Fortitude does not glorify suffering for its own sake. It does not tell the soul to seek pain. But it recognizes that life requires participation. Worms dig. Birds build. Every living thing bears some form of care.

To refuse all struggle is to refuse life itself.

Fortitude is linked to the strength of the lion and the humanity of Christ. This is not harsh strength. It is incarnate strength. It is the courage to remain faithful inside the conditions of earthly life.

Fortitude teaches us that grace does not remove all effort. Grace gives us strength to endure with love.

#### Living This Vision

There are times when rest is holy.

But there is also a kind of avoidance that disguises itself as rest. It says, “I should not have to face this.” It hides its hands. It covers its feet. It withdraws from the work of becoming whole.

This vision invites gentle honesty.

Where are you tired because you need rest?  
And where are you tired because you are resisting the next faithful step?

Fortitude does not demand that you become fearless. It simply asks you to move with the strength you have.

One honest conversation.  
One small act of service.  
One necessary task.  
One prayer when you would rather disappear.

The soul becomes strong not by escaping life, but by meeting life with God.
