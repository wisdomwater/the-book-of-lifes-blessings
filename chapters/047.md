## Day 47 – The Restless Dog

> *I saw a creature that leapt and played, yet had no anchor within itself.*

Another figure rose from the fog—a restless presence, shifting and eager.

It took the form of a dog standing upright, its body animated with movement. Its hind legs were fixed upon the earth, held fast as though bound there. Yet its front paws stretched upward, as if reaching toward something higher.

But there was no stillness in it.

It moved constantly—wagging, circling, turning this way and that. Its attention flickered from one thing to another. It chased desires as they appeared, grasping whatever seemed pleasing in the moment.

It hunted not with purpose, but with impulse.

Its actions seemed almost playful, even harmless at first glance. But beneath this liveliness was a deeper instability. It spoke as though it knew the way of truth, as though it could gesture toward higher things—but it did not walk in them.

Its movements were scattered.

Its desires ruled it.

And so it led many astray, not through force, but through distraction.

For those who followed it became like it—unsettled, unfocused, drawn into whatever passing impulse arose.

#### What This Vision Means

Petulance is the soul unanchored.

It is not always loud or destructive. Often, it appears as restlessness, impulsiveness, or a constant turning toward whatever feels good in the moment.

It mimics life, but lacks depth.

The image of the dog standing between earth and heaven reveals a divided state. There is a faint awareness of something higher—but no commitment to it. The soul gestures toward truth without grounding itself in it.

This creates instability.

Without rootedness, even good intentions become scattered. The soul becomes easily swayed, moving from desire to desire, never resting long enough to be transformed.

Petulance is dangerous not because it appears evil, but because it prevents depth. It keeps the soul on the surface of life.

#### Living This Vision

There is a kind of exhaustion that comes from always moving.

Always scrolling.  
Always reacting.  
Always chasing the next thing.

This vision speaks gently into that state.

Where in your life are you being pulled by impulse rather than guided by intention?

Where are you reaching upward—but not standing firmly in what you know is true?

The invitation here is not harsh discipline, but quiet steadiness.

To pause.  
To choose.  
To remain.

Spiritual life grows not through constant motion, but through rooted presence.

You do not need to chase what is good.

You can stand in it.
