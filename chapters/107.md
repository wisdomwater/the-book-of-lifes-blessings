## Day 107 - Against the Spirit of Strife

> *I heard the voice of contention sneer at every guide and every law, as though no one should ever yield, and I saw that peace is a form of strength.*

Then I understood more clearly the spirit that loves dispute.

It speaks with mockery toward any voice that would guide, restrain, or judge.  
It says:  
“Who made you ruler over us?  
What authority do you have to correct us?  
Are you not like us?  
Why should we be turned this way or that, as if we were a wheel under your hand?”

This voice resists teaching.  
It despises discipline.  
It does not want a law above itself,  
nor a wisdom it did not invent.

It complains when told not to fight.  
It bristles when urged toward restraint.  
It rejects correction whether spoken openly or quietly.

So I saw that the desire to contend does not merely love quarrels.  
It also refuses order.  
It suspects every true judgment.  
It claims that law is only the will of the strong,  
and that guidance is only disguised domination.

But the living truth showed me another way.

Those who wish to save their souls  
do not walk in these toils of war.  
They do not feed themselves on argument for its own sake.  
They move with peace in word and deed.  
They bend their will toward justice.

Their strength is not loud.  
Their firmness is not cruel.  
They do not surrender truth,  
but neither do they make conflict their home.

For peace is not weakness.  
It is the ordering of the soul under God.

And justice is not the fever of winning.  
It is the steady willingness to do what is right  
without scorning the neighbor.

So the soul that would live  
must learn not only how to speak,  
but how to refrain.  
Not only how to resist evil,  
but how to refuse the vanity of strife.

#### What This Vision Means

This passage deepens the earlier vision of contention by showing its hidden root: refusal of any authority beyond the self. The contentious soul does not simply disagree. It resents being guided, corrected, or bounded.

That is why the language here is defensive and suspicious. It hears wisdom as control. It hears judgment as insult. It hears restraint as oppression. In this state, the soul cannot receive truth, because it has already decided that correction is an enemy.

The contrast is important. Hildegard does not describe holiness as passivity. The peaceful soul still seeks justice. It still acts. But it is not animated by combativeness. It does not confuse righteousness with perpetual resistance.

Peace here is ordered strength. It is the capacity to remain aligned with truth without becoming possessed by argument. This is a mature spiritual freedom: to be governed by God rather than by the instinct to push back at every limit.

#### Living This Vision

There are moments when correction stings,  
even when it is needed.

Something in us rises quickly:  
Who are you to tell me?  
Why should I listen?  
Why should I yield?

This vision asks us to notice that reaction.

Not every authority is holy.  
Not every judgment is wise.  
But when we cannot receive any guidance at all,  
the soul grows defensive and lonely.

Sometimes peace begins with a pause.  
Not immediate agreement—  
just the refusal to turn everything into a fight.

Can I listen without hardening?  
Can I disagree without contempt?  
Can I pursue justice without feeding my ego?

The person who is always ready for battle  
often cannot recognize when love is trying to help.

Let peace become a discipline.  
Let teachability become strength.  
Let your zeal for what is right  
be cleansed of the need to conquer.
