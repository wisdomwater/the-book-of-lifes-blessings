## 86 - The Settled Waters

> *What no longer reaches begins to receive. The heart at rest discovers it is already held.*

I saw a vast body of water stretching farther than sight could follow. Once it had been stirred by many winds, pulled endlessly in different directions by currents that never seemed to settle. But now the water had grown still. Its surface rested quietly beneath the open sky, smooth as a mirror that no longer strained to reflect what passed above it.

Light rested upon the water without distortion.

Nothing within it reached beyond its own shores. It did not search anxiously for more streams to satisfy itself, nor did it resist what entered its depths. It simply received what was given. There was a quiet sufficiency in its stillness, as though the water had ceased fighting against its own nature and had finally become spacious enough to rest within itself.

And beneath the calm surface, life continued moving gently.

Creatures drifted through hidden currents without fear or urgency. Nothing chased. Nothing grasped. Everything moved within a deeper rhythm that did not need to force itself forward. The stillness was not emptiness. It was fullness without striving.

And I understood then that what is always reaching cannot fully see what is already here.

The restless soul remains convinced that peace waits somewhere beyond the present moment—just beyond the next achievement, possession, experience, or reassurance. But when the heart becomes still enough to receive life as it is, another awareness slowly appears: the deeper fullness we seek has often been quietly holding us all along.

**Living the Vision**

Much of human life is shaped by subtle forms of reaching. We move toward the next task, the next solution, the next source of comfort, the next version of ourselves we imagine will finally feel complete. Often this movement becomes so constant that we barely notice it anymore. The mind quietly whispers: something is still missing.

And so the soul leans forward again.

Yet beneath this endless striving there is often profound exhaustion. The nervous system grows weary from believing it must constantly secure peace, meaning, or worth through effort alone. Even good desires can become burdens when they are driven by fear that the present moment is not enough.

The contemplative life invites another way of being.

Not passivity. Not resignation. But a gentle return to presence. We begin learning how to pause without immediately filling the silence. We allow ourselves to rest, even briefly, from compulsive reaching. And in those moments, something subtle begins to change. The urgency softens. Space opens within us.

We begin noticing what has quietly sustained us all along—breath, beauty, kindness, grace, simple existence itself.

This does not mean desire disappears. Human longing remains part of our aliveness. But desire becomes re-centered. Instead of endlessly grasping outward, it learns how to receive what is already here. The soul discovers that peace is not always found by acquiring more, but by awakening more deeply to the life already being given.

And in that awakening, a quieter kind of fullness emerges.

Not dramatic.

Not possessive.

Simply steady enough to rest within.

**Breath Prayer**

**Inhale:** I rest in what is

**Exhale:** I receive enough
