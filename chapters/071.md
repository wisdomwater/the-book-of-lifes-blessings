## Day 71 — The Bitter Leopard

> *A hardened heart rejects what could heal it.*

I saw another image—swift and fierce.

It was like a leopard.

Its presence was sharp, unyielding.  
It spoke with contempt:

“I care nothing for courage or truth.  
If words of faith disturb me, I will cast them aside.  
I will twist them as I please.”

Its voice carried a bitterness that refused correction.

And from the storming cloud, a voice replied:

“You are harsh and dangerous.  
You refuse the commands of God  
and choose to remain in your bitterness.”

But then the voice softened, like rain falling gently on dry ground:

“I am generosity.

I come as rain to nourish,  
as dew to refresh,  
as balm to heal,  
as medicine to comfort.

I remain where grace is welcomed.  
I bring life where there was hardness.”

The contrast was clear.

One rejected truth.  
The other poured itself out freely.

One clung to bitterness.  
The other became a source of healing.

#### What This Vision Means

This image reveals a resistance within the human heart—the tendency to reject what challenges or corrects us.

Bitterness often disguises itself as strength. It resists change, dismisses truth, and protects itself from anything that might soften it.

But in doing so, it cuts itself off from healing.

Generosity, in contrast, is not only about giving material things. It is a posture of openness. It allows grace to flow—into oneself and outward toward others.

The imagery of rain, dew, balm, and medicine shows that true spiritual life is restorative. It does not force—it nourishes.

Where bitterness isolates, generosity reconnects.

This vision invites a question:  
Am I resisting what could heal me?

#### Living This Vision

Notice where you feel defensive.

When something challenges you—  
do you immediately push it away?

Bitterness often begins in small places.  
A slight.  
A wound.  
A disappointment.

Left unattended, it hardens.

But you are not called to stay there.

Try this:

When you feel resistance, pause.  
Let the moment breathe.

Ask yourself—  
is there something here for me?

You do not have to accept everything blindly.  
But you can remain open.

Generosity begins inwardly—  
as a willingness to receive truth, even when it is uncomfortable.

From there, it flows outward.

Like rain.  
Like dew.

Quietly restoring what has become dry.
