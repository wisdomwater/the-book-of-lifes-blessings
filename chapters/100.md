## 100 - The Rooted Delight

> *What remains begins to nourish. What is held in love becomes full.*

I saw a quiet garden resting beneath the calmness that follows a long wandering wind. The air had once moved restlessly through the land, carrying fragments in every direction, never settling long enough for anything to deepen. Everything had felt scattered then—touched briefly, abandoned quickly, unable to remain anywhere long enough to become whole.

But now a stillness had come.

The ground lay open and softened, as though the earth itself had grown ready to receive again. At the center of the garden a small spring rose steadily from hidden depths. Its waters were clear and unhurried, flowing without strain or urgency. It did not rush outward trying to fill every empty place at once. It simply gave what it carried, moment by moment, with quiet constancy.

Around this spring, roots reached downward.

They did not spread anxiously across the surface searching everywhere at once. They deepened where the water was. And because they remained near the source, life began unfolding naturally around them. Leaves opened slowly toward the light. Branches strengthened. Fruit appeared in its own season—without force, without spectacle.

Nothing in the garden hurried.

Nothing wandered aimlessly.

Everything rested within a gentle fullness that came not from excess, but from abiding close to what truly nourished it.

And I understood then that there is a form of joy the soul cannot discover through endless movement alone.

Some forms of delight only grow where we learn how to remain.

**Living the Vision**

Many people spend much of their lives moving from one thing to another in search of fulfillment. New experiences, new distractions, new goals, new relationships, new ideas—always hoping the next thing will finally quiet the ache beneath the surface. At first this movement can feel exciting, even freeing. Yet over time the soul often grows weary from never staying long enough for deeper roots to form.

What is constantly abandoned cannot fully nourish us.

The contemplative life invites a quieter rhythm. It asks us to notice what consistently brings life to the soul—not what merely stimulates or distracts us for a moment, but what genuinely deepens peace, clarity, tenderness, and presence over time. Often these sources of nourishment appear surprisingly simple: prayer, stillness, meaningful work, honest relationships, beauty, service, rest, truth, love quietly practiced in ordinary days.

These things may not always feel dramatic.

But they sustain life from within.

To remain with what nourishes us requires patience. It asks the soul to resist the constant temptation to flee toward novelty whenever discomfort or restlessness arises. Yet something beautiful happens when we stay long enough for roots to deepen beneath the surface of our lives. Joy becomes less dependent upon changing circumstances. Peace grows steadier. Love matures into something more spacious and enduring.

And gradually we discover that delight itself can become rooted.

Not a fleeting happiness that disappears with the next shifting wind, but a quiet gladness flowing from connection with what is true and lasting. Like a spring hidden beneath the garden, this deeper joy continues giving life because it remains close to its source.

The soul becomes full not by grasping endlessly for more, but by abiding deeply within what already carries living water.

**Breath Prayer**

**Inhale:** Delight me in You

**Exhale:** Root me in what is true
