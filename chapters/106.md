## Day 106 - The Well of Quarrels

> *I saw a deep well wrapped in black fire and storm, and I understood how the love of conflict throws the soul into confusion again and again.*

After this I saw evil spirits crying out to the souls of men,  
challenging them, provoking them,  
urging them never to yield, never to rest, never to let peace stand.

They taught the desire to contest.  
They stirred up the love of quarrel.

Then I saw a well of tremendous depth.  
Around it was black and fiery fog.  
A violent wind blew through it, carrying flame.  
And within both fog and wind were dreadful worms.

In these torments were souls who had delighted in contention.

Those who sought disputes not only in speech but in action  
were cast into the fire, driven by the burning wind,  
thrown down into the well,  
lifted again from the well into the wind,  
and tormented also by the worms.

But those who had fought only with words,  
and had not carried their quarrels into deeds,  
suffered the other torments  
but were not hurled into the well.

I understood this clearly.

The black and fiery fog tormented them  
because they had brought confusion to others.  
The painful wind burned them  
because they had pursued strife with cruelty.  
The well received them  
because their fighting had cast others down into distress.  
And when they rose only to return again to wickedness,  
they were thrown back once more into torment.

The worms afflicted them  
because arrogance had accompanied their contentious spirit.

Then I heard the voice from the living light:  
“What you see is true.  
Those who wish to turn away from these spirits  
must discipline themselves according to the measure of their fault.”

And I saw that the desire to contest  
is a failure of holy stillness.

It cannot bear patience.  
It does not endure insult.  
It seeks out quarrelsome company.  
It attacks with words.  
It leaves no room for peace.

Those who love strife imitate the devil,  
who could not bear the beauty of others  
and desired to exalt himself above them.

#### What This Vision Means

This vision shows contention as restless pride. It is not the same as honest disagreement or the courage to speak truth. It is the inward appetite for friction—the need to resist, dominate, provoke, or win.

The deep well is a powerful image. It reveals how conflict can become cyclical. A person stirs up turmoil, falls into confusion, rises again, and then repeats the same pattern. The soul becomes trapped in agitation.

The difference between those who fought in words and those who fought in deeds matters here. Speech alone already wounds. But when contentiousness becomes action—when it creates real harm, division, or cruelty—it deepens the soul’s descent.

The root of this vice is the loss of stillness. Holy stillness is not passivity. It is inward peace before God. Without that peace, the soul becomes reactive and easily inflamed.

To love contention is to lose harmony with truth, with neighbor, and with oneself.

#### Living This Vision

Some people argue because they care.  
But some arguments do not come from care at all.

They come from agitation.  
From pride.  
From the need to push, provoke, or prevail.

This vision invites us to examine not only what we say,  
but the spirit in which we say it.

Do I want clarity,  
or do I want victory?

Do I seek peace,  
or do I secretly enjoy the fire?

Contention feeds on speed.  
Stillness weakens it.

Pause before responding.  
Let the first wave pass.  
Do not give every irritation a voice.

Sometimes the holiest thing is not winning the exchange,  
but refusing the quarrel.

Peace does not mean pretending nothing is wrong.  
It means speaking from groundedness rather than heat.

A still soul can tell the truth  
without throwing another into the well.
