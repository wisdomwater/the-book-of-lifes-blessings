## Day 1 — The Voice That Calls

> *The divine voice does not come because we are strong.
> It comes because we are willing to listen.*

Hildegard begins not with power, but with weakness.

She calls herself what she believes she truly is—a simple mortal. Yet the Living Light had already been visiting her for many years. Since childhood she had seen things others could not see: living symbols, mysterious forms, truths about creation, and answers for those who came seeking wisdom.

These revelations were not easy gifts.

Often the visions left her exhausted. Her body grew weak beneath their weight. For years she carried a heaviness that she could not explain, as if the human frame struggled to contain what heaven had shown her.

Eight long years passed in this frailty.

Then, when she had reached sixty years of age, a powerful vision came again. It burned with such intensity that she suffered beneath it for five more years.

At last, in the year 1158, when she was sixty-one years old, something new happened.

A voice spoke from heaven.

It did not flatter her. It reminded her who she was—and who she was not.

“You who have received these revelations since childhood, not by the eyes of the body but by the Spirit of the Lord: now speak what you see and hear.”

The voice explained that the earlier visions had been like milk—gentle nourishment given to a child. But now stronger food was being given. The time had come to speak more clearly.

Then the voice gave a command that removed every doubt:

“Speak not from yourself, but from Me. Write not from yourself, but from Me.”

Hildegard did what many would do in such a moment.

She wanted to hide.

She wished that no one would discover her. She wished to disappear from the burden of such a calling.

But the voice continued to speak.

And so, trembling yet obedient, she began to write.

#### What This Vision Means

The opening of this book reveals something essential about spiritual revelation: it rarely comes to those who feel ready.

Hildegard does not describe herself as powerful, enlightened, or worthy. She describes weakness, illness, and reluctance. The visions weigh upon her body and soul. Yet it is precisely through this fragile vessel that the Living Light chooses to speak.

This pattern appears again and again in the spiritual life. Divine wisdom does not emerge from human confidence but from surrender.

The voice also describes a progression: first milk, then solid food. Early spiritual understanding is often simple and comforting. But as the soul matures, deeper truths emerge—truths that require courage, humility, and perseverance.

The most striking command is this: *“Speak not from yourself, but from Me.”*

This is the heart of prophetic speech. The true messenger does not invent the message. They become a listening vessel.

Hildegard’s reluctance is not failure. It is evidence of humility. The soul that knows its own smallness becomes a clearer window for divine light.

#### Living This Vision

Most people imagine that spiritual purpose arrives with clarity and confidence.

In reality, it often arrives with hesitation.

You may feel a quiet inner call—to speak truth, to create something meaningful, to encourage someone, or to walk a path that feels larger than your own strength. Yet the first instinct is often the same one Hildegard felt: *to hide.*

We doubt ourselves. We wonder if we are worthy. We fear being seen.

But the voice in this vision offers a gentle correction: the work is not ours alone.

We are not asked to produce light.

We are asked to carry it.

Your task is not perfection. It is listening. It is responding to the quiet invitations that arise within your conscience and spirit.

The courage required is smaller than we think. Often it begins with a single act of obedience—writing one sentence, speaking one truth, taking one step.

Hildegard did not feel ready.

She simply began to write.
