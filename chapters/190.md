## Day 190 - The Gentle Teacher

> *Correction must be guided by wisdom. The soul needs both truth and mercy, each given in the right measure.*

While a person lives in the world, they may choose good or evil.

Therefore, while still in the body, they may take up repentance for their sins, according to wise counsel. In this way, being cleansed from the filth of sin, they may be spared the bitterness of punishment and discover the sweetness of true life.

But the one who guides another must discern carefully.

The teacher must consider the strength and weakness of each soul, the nature of each disciple, the condition of each sin, and even the intention with which it was committed.

Each penitent must be understood according to their nature. Each repentance must be measured according to the sin and the heart that bears it.

The teacher must keep the rod of correction nearby.

Some hearts are hard and rough. If they are simply permitted to follow their own will, they may become rebellious and destructive. Such souls need firm correction.

But others still shine with a small light.

To these, the teacher must speak gently. If they are handled harshly, their light may be extinguished, and what was beginning to heal may become worse.

So the teacher must know the softness of Jacob and the roughness of Esau.

Jacob erred, yet there was good will in him. Esau erred with a hardened will. God loved Jacob’s good intention and rejected Esau’s malice.

So the teacher must draw near to the one who errs with good will, but firmly correct the one who errs with harmful intent.

If someone has sinned gravely and desires repentance, the teacher must help. They must look at the wounds and consider what care is needed.

But if the teacher rages unjustly against good and righteous disciples, that teacher becomes like those who stoned Stephen.

If the teacher wounds the innocent and drains their good works, that teacher becomes like a wolf.

If the teacher hides the evil of the corrupt, that teacher becomes like a thief.

A good teacher watches by day and guards by night.

With those who do good, the teacher praises with the sound of the harp and rejoices in the good.

Good teachers are like fresh air.

They govern with discretion and just correction.

#### What This Vision Means

This passage offers a vision of spiritual guidance.

Correction is necessary, but it must be wise. Discipline without discernment can destroy what it means to heal. Gentleness without truth can allow harm to grow.

The teacher must see the person, not merely the fault. Strength, weakness, intention, and wound all matter.

The contrast between harshness and tenderness is important. Some souls need firm restraint. Others need protection for the small light still burning in them.

The good teacher is like fresh air—not suffocating, not scorching, but life-giving and clear.

#### Living This Vision

We all become teachers at times.

Parents, friends, leaders, coworkers, mentors—we all respond to the failures of others.

This vision asks us to slow down before correcting.

Am I seeing the person clearly?

Is this a moment for firmness or tenderness?

Am I protecting the vulnerable, or only defending my own frustration?

True correction serves healing. It does not humiliate. It does not excuse harm. It seeks the restoration of the soul.
