## Day 123 - The Grass Crown of Vainglory

> *I saw vainglory dressed in darkness yet crowned with grass, carrying tender things in its hands and turning them toward itself, longing not only to do but to be admired for doing.*

Then I was shown the meaning  
of the third image.

It is Vainglory.

It comes after envy,  
for when people cannot bear the success of others,  
they begin to hunger for glory themselves.

It looked like a man,  
because it lives in the desires  
and longings of the flesh.

Its hands were covered with hair,  
for it directs its works in a beastlike way,  
though by reason it ought to be governed  
with human understanding.

Its legs and feet were like those of a crane,  
because it moves toward false heights.

It stands on instability,  
believing itself lifted up,  
yet the height it seeks  
is only empty appearance.

On its head it wore a twisted cap of grass.

This is because those who live by vainglory  
prize worldly things that fade quickly—  
green for a moment,  
then dry and gone.

It wore a black garment,  
for this vice does not live in true life  
but is clothed in the darkness of unbelief  
and the death that follows from it.

In its right hand it held a green twig,  
and in the left  
it carefully studied certain flowers.

So do those who live in vainglory  
display spiritual works in empty presumption,  
as though they possessed  
the strength of heavenly things.

At times, wishing to win the favor of the world,  
they present earthly deeds  
as if these bloomed by their own virtue and honesty.

They fix their attention there.  
They expend their effort there.  
They want their actions  
to be glorified  
through the vanity of boasting.

Vainglory does not simply want to do.  
It wants to be seen doing.

It does not simply want the flower.  
It wants the eyes of others  
to notice it in its hand.

And so the fear of God rises against it,  
exposing how ugly it is beneath its show.

#### What This Vision Means

Vainglory is the craving to be admired for what one is or does. It can attach itself to almost anything—talent, beauty, wisdom, morality, suffering, even humility or devotion.

The grass crown is especially sharp imagery. Vainglory wears what is green for a moment but soon withers. It crowns itself with what cannot last. The black garment shows the hidden emptiness beneath outward display.

The twig and flowers reveal something subtle: vainglory often handles good things. It may be very near beauty, virtue, or noble work. But it cannot leave them facing God. It turns them into mirrors for the self.

This is why vainglory is spiritually dangerous. It can feed on genuinely good actions and drain them of purity. The issue is not whether the action is outwardly impressive. The issue is where the heart secretly turns after the action is done.

The fear of God restores direction. It teaches the soul to care more for truth than appearance, more for fruitfulness than applause.

#### Living This Vision

Vainglory is familiar to almost everyone.

It appears when you do something kind  
and immediately wonder who noticed.

When you speak wisely  
and replay how you sounded.

When you help, create, endure, or pray—  
and then hunger to be admired for it.

There is no need for shame in noticing this.  
Only honesty.

Ask yourself gently:  
Would I still do this  
if no one ever knew?

That question has a way of cleansing the heart.

You do not have to stop doing beautiful things.  
You only need to return their beauty to God.

Practice hidden goodness when you can.  
Let some acts go unseen.  
Let some offerings be secret.

Grass dries quickly.  
Applause fades.  
But what is done in love before God  
remains alive.
