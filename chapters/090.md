## Day 90 – The Bitter Leopard

> *After excess comes hardness, and the soul grows sharp where it once overflowed.*
> *What was meant for nourishment becomes a bitterness that wounds.*

I saw a presence that followed gluttony like winter follows summer. It came after fullness had gone too far, when abundance had turned heavy and the spirit had grown dull beneath it.

This figure was like a leopard—restless, harsh, and filled with bitterness. Its movements were abrupt, its nature divided. In its words and in its deeds, there was a roughness that cut and scraped. It did not receive life with gratitude but hardened itself, as though every gift were a burden instead of grace.

And I understood: when a person takes in more than is needed—more food, more comfort, more indulgence—the soul does not become soft. It becomes coarse. A hidden bitterness begins to grow, and the heart turns away from God, no longer seeing Him as giver, but as something to resist.

Then I heard a living voice speak through the prophet:

“If you open your heart to the hungry,
and comfort the afflicted,
your light will rise in the darkness,
and your night will become like midday.”

I saw the soul like wind moving across grass—unseen, yet stirring life. I saw it like dew, gently resting, and like rain, bringing growth.

The voice continued:  
Be wind to the poor.  
Be dew to the abandoned.  
Be rain to the needy.  
Feed those who hunger—not only with bread, but with wisdom and care.

And when a person gives like this, something changes.

Their light begins to rise from within the darkness of sin. The shadows lose their power. Even what once obscured them begins to serve their transformation. Their sins fade like the afternoon light, dissolving into nothing.

Then the soul is filled—not with excess, but with clarity. It shines like a clear sky. Its strength returns. Even the bones—once weakened—are made firm again, destined for a life that cannot decay.

#### What This Vision Means

Roughness is what follows when desire is allowed to rule without wisdom. It is not simply physical excess, but a deeper spiritual imbalance—taking in without giving, consuming without gratitude, receiving without reverence.

At first, excess feels like pleasure. But over time, it produces a subtle bitterness. The soul becomes less responsive, less tender. It begins to resist rather than receive. Gratitude fades. Hardness grows.

The leopard image reveals this inner state: sharp, reactive, easily provoked. A divided nature emerges—one that speaks of goodness but acts with harshness.

The remedy is not denial, but transformation through generosity.

The vision shifts from consumption to flow. The soul is meant to move like wind, dew, and rain—receiving from God and passing that life onward. When we give—especially to those in need—we realign with the true movement of the soul.

Light returns not by force, but by generosity.

And even past darkness begins to lose its hold.

#### Living This Vision

Notice where life has become excessive—not only in food, but in comfort, distraction, or control.

Ask gently:  
Has this made my heart softer… or harder?

Roughness often hides beneath irritation, impatience, or quiet dissatisfaction. It is the feeling that nothing is quite enough, even when there is abundance.

The invitation here is simple but powerful:  
Begin to give.

Offer your attention.  
Offer your presence.  
Offer kindness where it is not required.

Be like wind—lifting others quietly.  
Be like dew—bringing comfort without force.  
Be like rain—nourishing what is dry.

As you do, something within you will begin to soften again.

Light does not need to be chased.  
It returns when the soul begins to flow.
