## 13 - The Garden That Remains

> *Beauty passes through your hands. Love roots itself within you.*

A garden opened before me in quiet abundance, alive with softness and light. Branches stretched outward in every direction, heavy with blossoms unfolding gently beneath the warmth of the morning sun. Their colors moved delicately through the air—rose, gold, pale white, and violet—while petals drifted downward like a slow blessing, resting briefly upon the skin before returning again to the earth.

Beneath one flowering tree stood a figure gathering blossoms close against their chest. There was tenderness in the movement, yet also a quiet urgency, as though beauty might disappear if not held tightly enough. The air itself seemed full—rich with fragrance, warmth, and the fleeting fullness of a moment too beautiful to lose.

And yet, slowly, almost beyond notice at first, the blossoms began to change.

Their brightness softened. Their edges curled inward. One by one they loosened from the branches and fell gently to the soil below. Nothing violent happened. Nothing was taken away in cruelty. The garden simply continued moving according to its deeper rhythm—the ancient pattern of blooming and returning, arrival and release.

The branches eventually grew still. Not barren. Not empty. Only quieter than before.

And beneath the tree, something within the figure also began to change. The hands that had once reached outward to gather what was fading slowly opened. The gaze that had fixed itself upon what could be possessed softened inward, toward something more enduring that had been quietly growing all along.

For beneath the beauty of blossoms, there was another life hidden deeper still.

Roots.

Steady. Invisible. Patient beneath the surface.

A life not dependent upon preserving every passing flower, but upon remaining connected to the source from which beauty continually rises again.

And I understood then that love matures when it learns how to cherish without clinging.

**Living the Vision**

There are moments in life so beautiful that the heart instinctively wants to hold them forever. A season of closeness with someone we love. A feeling of inner clarity. A version of ourselves that once felt vibrant and alive. The soul recognizes beauty naturally and longs to remain near it. There is tenderness in this longing. It comes from love.

Yet many forms of suffering begin when we try to preserve what was never meant to remain unchanged.

We close our hands around moments already moving through time. We resist endings, transitions, aging, and transformation because part of us fears that if the outer form changes, the beauty itself will disappear. But life rarely allows us to keep blossoms exactly as they are. Seasons shift. Feelings evolve. People grow. Even the most radiant experiences pass through our hands eventually.

Spiritual maturity does not require becoming detached from beauty. It asks something gentler: that we learn how to receive beauty fully without demanding permanence from it. The soul slowly discovers that what it truly longs for is deeper than the passing form itself. Beneath every beautiful moment is a deeper life—the living source from which love, tenderness, joy, and meaning continue to arise.

This deeper life cannot always be seen immediately because it grows quietly, like roots beneath the soil. Yet it remains even when outward seasons change. What is rooted in love does not vanish simply because appearances shift. In many ways, it deepens.

And so the invitation is not to stop loving what is beautiful, but to love without grasping. To let the heart open rather than tighten. To trust that what is truly sacred cannot be lost merely because it changes form. The blossoms may fall, but the garden remains alive beneath the surface, preparing quietly for another season of flowering.
