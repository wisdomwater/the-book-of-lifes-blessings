## Day 153 - Lift Me from the Mud

> *Hardness sinks the soul into mud, but repentance cries out for the mercy that can lift us again.*

Then I saw the sixth image: Stubbornness of Mind.

It followed concern for earthly things, because when people are consumed by worldly anxieties, their hearts often grow hard. They lose reverence for God. They speak and act as if God were not present. They no longer seek the tenderness of mercy.

This image was shaped like a buffalo.

It was heavy, rough, and forceful. It showed how stubbornness makes the heart harsh. Those ruled by it rise into a false security. They do not comfort others. They do not honor the goodness or wisdom that may live in another person.

They charge forward with sharp words.

They wound with bitter works.

They do not shelter. They do not protect. They spread fear wherever they can.

Then Repentance answered this vice.

Repentance called the soul away from hardness. It urged humanity to long again for divine things and to trust that God can draw us out of the storms of evil spirits.

And David’s prayer rose like a cry from the depths:

“Draw me out of the mud, and do not let me sink. Deliver me from those who hate me and from deep waters. Do not let the flood overwhelm me. Do not let the abyss swallow me.”

And I understood:

The soul asks God to lift it from the mud of its own corruption. It confesses that the flesh, made from earth, has become heavy with sin. What was meant to carry the fragrance of good works has become a place where harmful things crawl.

Yet the prayer does not end in despair.

It asks for grace.

“Lift me by Your mercy. Let the sweet perfume of virtue rise where there has been stench. Deliver me from the waters that drown. Do not let death close its mouth over me.”

The one whom God hears is blessed.

For God gives the soul the desire to seek what is worth finding.


#### What This Vision Means

Stubbornness is hardness that has forgotten tenderness.

The buffalo image reveals brute force without compassion. This vice does not listen. It does not bend. It charges ahead, convinced of its own rightness.

But beneath stubbornness is often fear. The hardened heart has learned to protect itself through control, anger, or refusal. It would rather become rough than vulnerable.

Repentance is the healing virtue because it softens what has become rigid.

David’s prayer gives language to the soul that knows it cannot free itself. The mud represents the condition of being stuck—not only in obvious sin, but in patterns that cling to us.

The deep waters represent overwhelm. The abyss represents the fear of being swallowed completely.

Yet the prayer trusts mercy more than failure.

Repentance begins when the soul stops pretending it is standing on solid ground and asks to be lifted.


#### Living This Vision

Hardness can feel like strength.

It can feel like clarity, conviction, or self-protection. But if it makes us cruel, dismissive, or unable to receive correction, it is not strength.

It is mud.

This vision invites a courageous prayer:

God, where have I become hard?

Not to condemn yourself.
Not to collapse into shame.
But to become soft enough to be healed.

Notice your sharp words.
Notice where you refuse to listen.
Notice where fear has dressed itself as certainty.

Then ask to be lifted.

Repentance is not self-hatred.

It is the willingness to be rescued.

The mercy of God does not despise the soul in the mud.

It reaches in.
