## 99 - The First Green

> *What feels bound can gently loosen. What seems lifeless may begin again.*

I saw a tree standing in the quietness of early light. Its branches were still and bare, woven tightly around themselves as though holding something they could not yet release. There was tension within the wood—a silent endurance shaped by long seasons of cold and waiting. Near the trunk stood a figure almost hidden among the branches, so close to the tree that its stillness seemed to belong to both of them.

Nothing moved at first.

The air itself felt heavy, suspended in a kind of sorrowful quiet. It was the stillness that comes when life has withdrawn deep beneath the surface, when nothing outward seems capable of changing. The tree did not appear dead, but it no longer knew how to open.

Then a gentle warmth began to gather.

It did not arrive dramatically from above. It seemed to rise from within the stillness itself, soft as dawn slowly entering the earth after a long winter night. The warmth did not force the branches apart or demand immediate transformation. It simply remained near.

And slowly, almost invisibly, something began to loosen.

A small opening appeared where there had once been only tension. Deep within the wood, faint traces of green emerged—not yet leaves, not yet fullness of life, but the unmistakable beginning of awakening. The branches did not suddenly disappear. The tree did not become whole in an instant.

Yet it was no longer entirely closed.

Something living had begun moving quietly within it again.

And I understood then that even what feels bound by sorrow, fear, exhaustion, or grief may still carry hidden seeds of life waiting patiently beneath the surface—not for force, but for gentle return.

**Living the Vision**

There are seasons when sorrow settles deeply into the soul. It no longer feels temporary or passing, but woven quietly into the shape of daily life. The world loses some of its brightness. Hope narrows. Energy withdraws inward. We move through our days carrying a heaviness that words often fail to explain.

And in these seasons, it can seem as though nothing within us is changing.

Yet the contemplative life reminds us that stillness is not always emptiness.

Beneath even profound grief, something often remains quietly alive. A single breath that softens the body for a moment. A conversation that brings unexpected warmth. A memory that still carries tenderness. A small desire to continue. These movements may appear fragile, yet they matter deeply. They are signs that life has not fully disappeared from within us.

Healing rarely arrives through force.

The soul cannot be commanded into wholeness. Like trees awakening after winter, much of inner restoration happens slowly and invisibly before it can be seen outwardly. The heart loosens little by little. Space opens gradually where tension once remained fixed. We begin carrying sorrow differently—not because it vanishes immediately, but because life slowly begins growing alongside it.

This is why gentleness matters so much in times of heaviness.

We do not need to demand instant transformation from ourselves. Sometimes the deepest act of faith is simply remaining present long enough to notice the faint green beginning to emerge beneath the bark.

Not fully formed.

Not yet complete.

But real.

And where life is still present, even quietly, hope has not ended.
