## 96 - Where Your Feet Rest

> *You do not need to gather the whole world. You are already held where you stand.*

I saw a wide plain resting beneath the quiet light of morning. The air carried the scent of open earth after rain, and everything seemed softened by a gentle stillness. Across this open field a figure moved slowly, searching as it walked. Its hands reached constantly toward small things scattered along the ground—gathering, holding, then reaching again as though something essential might always remain just beyond its grasp.

And yet beneath the figure, the ground itself seemed strangely hidden.

Soft shadows covered the place where its feet should have rested. Though it moved across the earth, it did not truly feel where it stood. Its attention remained fixed upon what might still be gained, secured, or carried forward.

Then, not far away, there appeared a clearing.

The light rested there without movement—steady, warm, sufficient in itself. And within that quiet place, the earth could be clearly seen: firm beneath the light, open and waiting without demand. When the figure slowed, even slightly, something within it began to change.

The hands lowered.

The reaching paused.

And for the first time, the figure felt the ground beneath its feet—solid, present, real. Nothing new had been gathered. Nothing additional had been earned or secured. Yet something deeper had been received.

A quiet sufficiency.

It was as though the earth itself whispered gently: you are already being carried.

And within that realization, the endless reaching softened into rest.

### Living the Vision

Much of human life becomes organized around the fear that what we have, what we are, or where we stand may not be enough. So the mind reaches constantly toward what might provide greater security—more preparation, more achievement, more certainty, more control, more reassurance against the future’s uncertainty.

Often these movements appear reasonable, even wise.

And yet beneath them there is frequently a quieter fear: the fear that if we stop reaching, we may discover emptiness beneath us.

The contemplative life invites us to pause long enough to notice another possibility.

There are moments—often very simple ones—when the soul becomes aware of what is already present. A breath entering the body without effort. Light through a window. The feeling of standing upon solid ground. The quiet companionship of another person. The ordinary gifts sustaining life moment by moment. These things do not remove all uncertainty, yet they gently remind us that existence itself is already carrying us in countless unseen ways.

This awareness changes the quality of desire.

The soul no longer grasps at life with the same desperation because it begins sensing that it is not abandoned to secure everything through effort alone. Desire softens into receptivity. Gratitude begins replacing panic. The body relaxes enough to inhabit the present moment instead of constantly fleeing toward the next imagined need.

And from this deeper grounding, we discover something surprising:

Enoughness is not always found by possessing more.

Sometimes it appears when we finally feel where our feet already rest.
