## Day 96 – The Tower of Ruin

> *The soul that turns from God builds its own fortress—strong, proud, and empty.*
> *What appears secure becomes the place of its collapse.*

I saw a towering structure rising high, like a fortress crowned with a great tower. It stood firm and unyielding, as though nothing could shake it.

This was the Ruin of Souls.

At the top of the tower were three windows, open and watchful. Through them, something looked out—something that used the powers of the soul: sense, intellect, and knowledge. Yet these were not turned toward God. They were turned outward, drawing souls toward destruction.

I understood that even the gifts meant to lead us to truth can be twisted to lead us away.

Below these windows stretched two arms across the fortress walls. They were bold, unafraid, reaching outward without reverence. They did not tremble before God. Instead, they carried a strange confidence—a deadly assurance rooted not in truth, but in emptiness.

Their sleeves were dark, hidden in shadow. But the hands were bare—and they burned.

I saw that the works of this ruin were exposed, stripped of holiness, yet filled with a fire of bitterness. It moved quietly, like a thief, slipping into hearts and leading them downward.

It desired nothing but this:  
to bring others where it already dwelt.

I felt the weight of it—this turning away, this refusal of God. It was not ignorance alone, but a willful hardness, a gnashing of teeth against the One who gives life.

Then a voice cried out through the prophet:

“Why do you dwell in a strange land?  
Why do you live among your enemies?  
You have forsaken the fountain of wisdom.”

And I saw how the soul, meant to be a dwelling of light, had become a dwelling place for its enemies. Its thoughts filled with the world alone. Its desires corrupted. Its memory clouded.

It slept within God’s law.  
It forgot its source.

And so it descended—slowly, steadily—into a place where no light remained.

#### What This Vision Means

The Tower of Ruin represents the soul that has turned fully away from God and become self-reliant in the deepest sense.

The three windows are especially striking. They show that the faculties of the soul—our ability to perceive, think, and understand—are not neutral. They can either open us to God or close us off from Him.

Here, they are misdirected.

The fortress itself symbolizes pride. It appears strong, secure, even admirable. But it is built apart from truth, and so it cannot lead to life. It becomes a prison rather than a refuge.

The burning hands reveal the nature of its actions—active, visible, yet driven by bitterness and separation.

This vision is not only about final destruction. It is about the gradual process by which the soul becomes estranged from its source—through neglect, forgetfulness, and misplaced trust.

To forsake wisdom is not simply to lack knowledge.  
It is to turn away from life itself.

#### Living This Vision

Ask yourself gently:

Where am I relying only on myself?

Where have I built structures of control, certainty, or pride that leave no room for God?

These structures may feel safe. They may even feel necessary. But if they are closed off from truth, they will slowly become places of isolation.

Notice also how you use your mind:
- Do your thoughts lead you toward clarity or confusion?
- Does your understanding deepen humility or strengthen separation?

The invitation is not to tear everything down in fear, but to open what has been closed.

Let light enter the windows.  
Let humility soften the walls.  
Let trust replace control.

The soul was not made to stand alone.  
It was made to dwell in wisdom.
