## Day 66 - Remedies Before the Abyss

> *Mercy gives warning before ruin. What can still be healed now should not be delayed until darkness teaches it by pain.*

The Living Light continued to speak, and I understood that repentance is not meant as cruelty, but as rescue.

Those who had sinned through anger, hatred, and bloodshed were shown different paths of correction. The remedies were not all the same, because the wounds were not all the same. The soul that had burned with hatred needed one kind of humbling. The soul that had acted in blindness needed another. The one who had killed from greed, or by hidden means, or from fear, or without intention, each bore a different burden and required a different healing.

I saw that true judgment is not careless.

It does not flatten all sins into one. It weighs intention, violence, blindness, presumption, and hardness of heart. It considers what was chosen and what was neglected. It looks not only at the act, but at the soul that performed it.

Some were called to severe fasting.

Some to lashes and sackcloth.

Some to solitude and withdrawal from human company.

Some to sleep in ashes.

Some to lighter measures, where the will had not fully consented to evil.

And I heard that those who had extinguished life within themselves, or had killed the children they carried or had borne, must not answer such darkness with denial, but with true and bitter repentance if they wished to be spared worse sorrows. Yet even here the vision spoke of remedy, not only condemnation. There remained, while life remained, a path of grief that could still become a path of healing.

Only of those who had given death to themselves was something different revealed. I did not see their purifying penance, for they had cut off the body by which penance is worked. Their act was shown as a terrible severing, a destruction without the comfort of repentance beforehand.

Then I understood this also: when a judge imposes penance, he must consider the frailty and strength of human nature. Penance is medicine, not spectacle. It must be fitted to the wound.

Blessed is the one who repents willingly, who comes forward before being forced, who begins now.

For repentance begun in this life and brought to completion in humility leads not into shame, but into eternal life.


#### What This Vision Means

This section reveals divine justice as deeply discerning. God does not judge mechanically. The soul’s intention, awareness, and condition matter. Hildegard presents repentance not as vengeance against the self, but as a fitting remedy for what has gone wrong.

The varying forms of penance symbolize this moral precision. A wound of hatred is not the same as a wound of fear. Blindness is not identical to greed. Hidden violence differs from impulsive harm. The soul is not healed by generic sorrow, but by truthful sorrow.

The instruction that judges must consider the weakness and strength of a person is especially important. This keeps penance from becoming harsh performance. True correction aims at restoration. It meets the person where they are while still honoring the seriousness of sin.

The vision also holds together two truths: some acts are dreadful beyond words, and yet while life remains, repentance remains possible. That tension is central. Divine mercy does not pretend evil is small. It insists that healing must go as deep as the wound.


#### Living This Vision

Most people are not called to literal sackcloth or prolonged fasting. But everyone is called to honest repair.

The question is simple: when you harm, how do you respond?

Do you minimize? Justify? Explain yourself quickly so you do not have to feel the weight of it?

Or do you let the truth reach you?

Real repentance has texture. It may look like confession, restitution, silence, changed behavior, accountability, or giving up what feeds the pattern. It should cost something, because harm always costs something. But its purpose is not self-destruction. Its purpose is to reopen the heart to truth.

And wisdom matters. Different wounds need different remedies. A harsh approach may crush where gentleness is needed. A soft approach may excuse what needs firmer correction.

Ask not only, “Am I sorry?” but “What medicine does this wound require?”

To repent is to agree to be healed, even when the healing is uncomfortable.
