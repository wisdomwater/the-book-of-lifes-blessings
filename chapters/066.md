## 66 - The Hidden Garden of Love

> *You are not sustained by being seen. You are sustained by what is real.*

I saw a wide and uncertain place where the ground felt soft beneath every step. The earth could not bear weight easily. Footsteps sank into it, and movement became slow and heavy, as though each person walking there carried an invisible burden pressing them downward. The more they struggled to rise above the surface, the deeper they seemed to settle into the unstable ground beneath them.

There was a constant reaching in that place.

A longing to be lifted higher, to be noticed, admired, affirmed. Every movement carried the subtle ache of wanting to be seen. Yet the striving itself seemed to weaken those who gave themselves to it. The attention they sought could never fully steady them. The ground beneath their lives remained uncertain because it depended so deeply upon the shifting gaze of others.

Then at the edge of this place, I noticed something entirely different.

There was a quiet garden hidden almost from sight. Nothing about it demanded attention. No voice announced its beauty. No crowd gathered around it. And yet life flourished there with extraordinary peace.

The soil was dark, rich, and firm beneath the roots growing quietly within it. Leaves unfolded slowly toward the light. Small flowers opened in silence. Everything in the garden seemed deeply rooted in something unseen yet entirely sufficient. No part of it strained to be admired. It simply grew where it had been planted.

And still, it flourished.

Not because others witnessed it.

But because it was nourished by what was real.

And I saw then that this hidden garden was not lesser for remaining unseen. It was free.

### Living the Vision

There is a deep human longing to be recognized. We want our efforts acknowledged, our goodness affirmed, our existence reflected back to us by the eyes of others. This desire is not entirely wrong. To be seen with love and understanding is part of what it means to be human.

And yet the soul can slowly become burdened when it depends too heavily upon being noticed.

We begin measuring ourselves through approval, comparison, or visibility. Even acts of kindness can become entangled with the hope of recognition. The heart grows restless, always aware of how it appears, how it is perceived, whether it is receiving enough affirmation to feel secure.

But there is another way of living.

A quieter path.

It begins when we allow some parts of our lives to remain hidden—not secretive out of fear, but protected from the constant need for display. A quiet act of generosity. A prayer spoken alone. A kindness offered without announcement. A moment of compassion no one else ever witnesses.

At first, this hiddenness can feel uncomfortable.

The ego fears invisibility because it equates being unseen with being unimportant. Yet slowly, something steadier begins to grow beneath the surface. The soul discovers a freedom deeper than praise. Actions rooted in love itself begin carrying their own quiet nourishment.

This hidden life becomes like deep roots beneath a garden.

Invisible perhaps, but essential.

And over time, we begin to understand that we are not sustained by how visible we are to the world. We are sustained by truth, by love, and by the quiet presence of the One who sees fully without requiring performance.

Nothing real is lost in hiddenness.

Sometimes the deepest growth happens there.
