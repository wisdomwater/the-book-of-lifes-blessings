## Day 70 — The Serpent’s Open Belly

> *What we justify, we eventually dwell inside.*

I saw a dreadful image in the darkness.

A serpent lay on its back, its body black and streaked with bruised and poisonous colors.  
Its eyes burned like fire.  
Its tongue hung out, restless.  
Its tail was cut short.

Its belly was torn open.

And within that hollow place, I saw a man lying as if in a cradle.  
He wore a pale garment, soft and bright like silk.  
But over it was a covering that resembled the serpent’s own skin.

He lifted his voice and said:

“God created all things.  
Why should I deny myself any of them?  
If they were not meant to be enjoyed, they would not exist.  
I would be a fool to refuse what is given.”

And I understood—this was the voice of Gluttony.

Then from the storm-filled cloud above, a voice answered:

“No one touches a harp carelessly,  
for if its strings are damaged, it will not sing.

But you, Gluttony, stretch the body beyond its measure.  
You fill it until its harmony is lost.

Where, then, is the wisdom God placed within you?”

The voice flowed like rain—gentle, steady, life-giving.

“I bring balance.  
I nourish without excess.  
I preserve the beauty formed by God’s hands.

When a person lives in moderation,  
their life becomes music.  
Their prayer rises like a harp.  
Their being becomes a song.”

But Gluttony knew nothing of this music.  
It swung wildly between excess and deprivation—  
stuffing and starving, never resting in truth.

And I saw:

The serpent’s belly had swallowed many.  
And what it could not hold, it defiled and cast out.

#### What This Vision Means

This vision reveals how easily desire can disguise itself as reason.

Gluttony does not appear as destruction at first. It presents itself as logic: “God made this—why not enjoy it fully?” But the deeper truth is that excess distorts what was meant to sustain life.

The serpent’s open belly shows that when we live without restraint, we begin to dwell inside what consumes us. The soul becomes shaped by the very appetite it serves.

The harp image is central. The human body and soul are meant to be tuned—to live in harmony. Too much strain, too much indulgence, or even extreme deprivation, damages that harmony.

True abstinence is not harshness. It is wisdom. It preserves clarity, restores balance, and allows the soul to become receptive again.

This vision is not against joy or nourishment. It is against losing oneself within them.

#### Living This Vision

Pay attention to what you reach for when you are restless.

Is it truly hunger?  
Or is it something deeper—fatigue, stress, emptiness?

There is a quiet difference between receiving and consuming.

You do not need to reject what is good.  
But you are invited to receive it with awareness.

Eat in a way that leaves you clear.  
Speak in a way that leaves you grounded.  
Rest in a way that restores.

Avoid the extremes—the cycles of overindulgence and harsh correction.  
These only deepen the imbalance.

Instead, listen for the quieter rhythm.

There is a way of living that feels like music—  
steady, alive, and whole.

Follow that.
