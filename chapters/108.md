## Day 108 - Born for Blessing

> *I saw a pit for those who believed themselves made for misfortune, and I heard that the nature of man is good, though hope may be buried beneath sorrow.*

After this I saw spirits crying out in defiance.  
They would not have God.  
They chose rebellion, and they whispered the same rebellion into human hearts.

They persuaded people toward unhappiness—  
not merely sorrow,  
but the belief that one is born under a curse,  
made for misery,  
formed for abandonment.

Then I saw a great pit, wide and deep.  
Within it burned sulfurous fire,  
and through it moved many worms.

In that place were souls  
who in earthly life did not fully trust God.  
When misfortune came upon them,  
they did not turn toward mercy.  
Instead they said their suffering proved  
that they had been born unlucky,  
born forsaken,  
born into an unhappy nature.

Because they did not trust God,  
they were held in the pit.

Because their distrust was shamefully embraced,  
the sulfurous fire burned them.

Because they attributed their wounds  
to some curse in their own created being,  
the worms tormented them.

Then the living light spoke,  
saying that those who would be freed from this vice  
must learn obedience and inward discipline,  
turning away from the spirits that preach despair.

And I understood this more deeply:

When some people meet repeated sorrow,  
their faith begins to fail.  
They say,  
“God does not want to help us.  
We were born in such unhappiness  
that no help can reach us.”

But this is not the truth.

The nature of man is good.

It is not human nature itself that is cursed,  
but the way a person departs from that goodness  
by letting untamed desire or despair  
lead the soul away from trust.

So I saw that those who have spoken such hopeless words  
must not remain in them.  
Let them sigh toward mercy.  
Let them admit their sin in losing hope.  
Let them ask again for grace.

For the soul is not born for ruin.  
It is made for God.

#### What This Vision Means

This vision addresses a very tender distortion of the soul: the belief that one’s suffering proves one is fundamentally doomed. Hildegard treats this not as simple sadness, but as a spiritual error born from distrust.

The pit signifies the enclosure of despair. The sulfur points to the corrosive shame that comes when a person begins to identify with misfortune. The worms suggest thoughts that burrow and repeat: *This is just what I am. I was made this way. Nothing good is meant for me.*

Against that lie comes a luminous sentence: **the nature of man is good.** This does not deny suffering, sin, or weakness. It means that our created being is not evil at its root. Despair lies when it treats woundedness as destiny.

Hope, then, is not naive optimism. It is a return to truth. We are not made for abandonment. We are made for grace, even if we have wandered far from trusting it.

#### Living This Vision

Many people carry a secret sentence inside:

This is just my lot.  
I was made for struggle.  
Others may find peace, but not me.

When life has wounded us again and again,  
that thought can feel almost holy in its honesty.

But this vision says: do not build your identity out of your pain.

You may be wounded.  
You may be weary.  
You may even feel surrounded by loss.

But you were not created for disgrace.

When despair speaks,  
answer gently but firmly:  
My nature is good, because it comes from God.

You do not need to pretend everything is fine.  
You only need to refuse the lie that suffering defines your essence.

Hope may begin very small.  
A whispered prayer.  
A deep sigh.  
A willingness to ask for help again.

That is enough to begin turning toward the light.
