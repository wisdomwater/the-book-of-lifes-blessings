## 2 - The Flowers That Wither

> The world dazzles with beauty that seems eternal.  
> Yet the light of truth reveals how quickly such beauty fades.

In the darkness I saw a vast multitude of lost souls. They were countless, like shadows without number. They moved away from the place where a hymn of praise was rising in the South. They wanted nothing to do with those who sang there. They refused every bond with them.

These souls followed a guide called the Seducer. They had given themselves to the one who once tried to overthrow Christ but who now had no true power. Yet they still listened to him.

And they cried out loudly in grief:

“Alas! Alas! What terrible deeds we have done. Our actions stole life from us and led us into death.”

Then I saw a cloud spreading from the North. It stretched into the darkness like a heavy shadow. This cloud carried no joy within it. No warmth touched it. The rays of the sun had never reached it.

Inside that cloud wandered many evil spirits. They moved restlessly, weaving traps for human souls. Yet even these spirits seemed uneasy when they remembered the Man whom I had seen before, the one who watched from the East and the South.

Then I heard the ancient serpent speaking to himself.

“I will gather all my strength,” he said. “I will prepare myself for battle and fight against my enemies with everything I possess.”

From his mouth poured a foul slime filled with every kind of impurity and vice. It fell upon humanity like corruption.

Mocking them, he sneered:

“They call themselves suns because of the light of their works. But I will turn them into darkness—ugly and harmful in the shadows.”

Then he breathed out a horrible mist. It spread across the earth like the blackest smoke. Within that fog thundered harsh voices that roared:

“No one should worship a god they cannot see. Why should anyone honor what they do not know?”

Within this choking fog I saw many forms that represented vices. Seven stood out clearly before me.

The first had the shape of a man. His skin was dark like deep shadow. He stood naked, wrapping his arms and legs around a great tree filled with many branches and beautiful flowers.

He eagerly gathered the blossoms in his hands and said:

“All the kingdoms of the world belong to me with their beauty and delight. Why should I fade when life is so full of vitality? Why should youth pass away when I can enjoy it now? Why should my eyes grow dim with age?

“If that happens, it would shame me.

“So I will embrace the beauty of this world while I can. I do not know what lies beyond this life, and the stories people tell about another world mean nothing to me.”

But as soon as he finished speaking, the great tree suddenly withered. Its branches shriveled. Its flowers collapsed.

The entire tree sank into the darkness.

And the figure who clung to it fell with it.

Then a voice answered from the stormy cloud:

“You are foolish if you believe that true life can be found in a spark that quickly turns to ash.

“You do not seek the life where youth never fades and old age never destroys beauty.

“You live in darkness, wrapped in fog. Like a worm hidden in the soil of human desire, you survive only for a moment. Then you dry up like hay and fall into ruin along with the flowers you claim to possess.

“But I stand as a pillar of heavenly harmony.

“I offer the joys of true life.

“I do not reject life itself. I only destroy what harms it. I am the mirror in which every virtue shines clearly. Yet you wander through darkness, and your hands produce nothing but rebellion.”

#### What This Vision Means

Hildegard’s vision reveals a spiritual struggle unfolding beneath the surface of ordinary life. The darkness represents the inner world where human choices slowly shape the soul.

The lost souls moving away from the hymn of praise symbolize people who turn away from the life of the spirit. They do not necessarily hate goodness—they simply distance themselves from it. They drift from the light.

The cloud from the North represents confusion and spiritual coldness. It is a place where the warmth of divine light has not reached the heart. In that space, deception thrives. The ancient serpent whispers an old argument that appears reasonable: *Only believe what you can see.*

This voice still echoes in every age.

The first vice—Worldly Love—clings to a tree full of flowers. The flowers symbolize pleasure, youth, beauty, success, and power. None of these things are evil in themselves. But when a person clings to them as the ultimate meaning of life, they become fragile idols.

The tree suddenly withering reveals a deep truth: the world’s beauty is temporary. What appears solid can collapse in a moment.

Divine Love answers not by condemning life, but by redirecting it. True life is not the denial of beauty—it is the discovery of a deeper beauty that does not decay.

The vision reminds us that the heart must choose where it will root itself: in the passing blossoms of the world, or in the enduring life that flows from God.

#### Living This Vision

Most of us recognize the voice of Worldly Love. It speaks whenever we say:

“I will think about deeper things later. Right now I just want to enjoy life.”

There is nothing wrong with enjoying beauty, laughter, or youth. These are gifts. But problems arise when we try to hold them forever.

Hildegard’s vision gently asks us a question: *What happens when the flowers fade?*

If our identity is built entirely on temporary things—appearance, success, pleasure, approval—we may feel the ground collapse beneath us when those things change.

But Divine Love offers another way of living.

Instead of clinging to the flowers, we learn to appreciate them without making them our foundation. We enjoy the world while remembering that it is not the final home of the soul.

This frees us.

We can delight in life without fear, because our deepest roots are planted in something that cannot wither. Faith becomes not an escape from the world, but a deeper way of seeing it.

The flowers are beautiful.

But the garden is larger than we imagined.
