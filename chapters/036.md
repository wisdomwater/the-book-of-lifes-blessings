## 36 - Strength That Does Not Collapse

> *You are allowed to bend. What holds you will not give way.*

I saw a living presence resting deeply within the earth itself—not distant or removed, but grounded like a quiet strength woven into the soil beneath all things. It carried firmness without hardness, stability without rigidity. The earth beneath it seemed able to receive everything: water moving in its course, wind settling softly through open air, life rising and returning in endless rhythms without strain or resistance.

There was a yielding within this strength that did not weaken it.

The ground did not cling tightly to what passed across it. It simply held. And because it held so deeply, movement could unfold freely upon it. Rivers could flow. Trees could bend in storms. Seasons could change without threatening the life beneath them.

Then I noticed a subtle trembling moving through the landscape.

It was the kind of trembling that comes when weight is finally felt—when pressure settles inward and hidden strain becomes visible. The earth itself seemed to bend slightly beneath what rested upon it. There was a soft giving way, a yielding to the burden it carried.

And yet nothing broke.

The movement itself was held safely within something deeper. It was as though the strength beneath the surface was never threatened by the strain above it. The bending did not lead to collapse because what supported it remained rooted far below the shifting pressures of the moment.

Then I saw lives passing through seasons of great inward weight.

People carrying sorrow, uncertainty, exhaustion, responsibility, grief. Times when the soul no longer felt capable of remaining strong in the ways it once had. Yet beneath these lives there remained a deeper holding—not forcing them upright, not demanding perfection, but quietly sustaining them through the pressure itself.

And in returning to this deeper ground, something within them steadied.

Not through resistance.

Not through hardening.

But through discovering a strength that could bend without breaking.

The bending itself became part of the standing.

And what endured was not what refused all weakness, but what remained rooted in something greater than itself.

**Living the Vision**

There are seasons when life presses inward with unexpected weight. Responsibilities accumulate. Grief lingers. The nervous system grows tired from carrying more than it knows how to hold. During these moments, many people begin fearing their own tenderness and fatigue. They believe that strength means remaining unaffected, unshaken, endlessly capable.

But the soul was never meant to survive through rigidity alone.

There is a kind of strength that eventually fractures because it refuses to bend. Yet there is another kind—a deeper resilience rooted not in force, but in connection. This strength allows a person to soften when needed, to rest when weary, to acknowledge trembling without shame.

Sometimes the holiest thing a person can do is stop pretending they are unbreakable.

For beneath our efforts to hold everything together, there is already a greater holding present. Divine love does not depend upon our constant self-sufficiency. It remains beneath us like deep earth beneath shifting weather, quietly sustaining us even when our own strength feels uncertain.

This does not mean suffering disappears or pressure no longer affects us. The bending is real. Weariness is real. Tears are real. Yet these experiences do not mean we are failing. Often they are invitations to root ourselves more deeply in what can truly sustain us.

Over time, many people discover that endurance is not found through hardening the heart against pain. It grows through remaining connected—to breath, to presence, to truth, to love, to God—even while life bends them in ways they did not expect.

And in that connection, something steady emerges.

Not perfection.

Not invulnerability.

But a quiet and living strength that does not collapse when life becomes heavy.

**Breath Prayer**

**Inhale:** You hold me steady

**Exhale:** I rest and remain
