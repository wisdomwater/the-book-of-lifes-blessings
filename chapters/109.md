## Day 109 - Scattered as Dust

> *I saw a vast sea mixed with sulfur and fire, and I understood how immoderation loses all center, reaching for everything and resting nowhere.*

Then I saw other spirits raging against the righteous angels of God.  
They boasted that just as the angels gave glory to their Lord,  
they too would honor their own.

These spirits stirred human beings toward excess.  
They whispered immoderation into every movement of life.

Then I saw a sea of extraordinary breadth and length.  
It was mixed with sulfur  
and churned violently with fire across its whole expanse.

In that sea were the souls  
of those who had lived without measure.

They had shown immoderation in speech,  
in action,  
in the motions of the body,  
in their thoughts,  
and in all the unchecked wrong things they allowed to grow.

Because they immersed themselves in contemptible excesses,  
they were plunged into the waters.

Because they forgot God  
and would not acknowledge Him,  
they were tormented by sulfur.

Because they neglected the law of God,  
they were punished by fire.

Then the voice from the living light came again,  
saying that whoever wishes to flee these spirits  
must take on the yoke of obedience  
and abstain from rich and indulgent foods.

And I understood the nature of this vice.

Immoderation does not live fully in heaven  
or fully on earth.  
It belongs nowhere.

Its every step moves toward uncertainty.

It says,  
“I would be lost if I could not explore everything,  
taste everything,  
push everything further.”

But it does not want peace.

It turns upon itself like a wheel in motion.  
It spins and spins,  
never arriving.

And as dust is scattered by the wind,  
so this soul is scattered—  
for it stores only excess.

The devil uses this restlessness  
to drag people toward confusion,  
just as he himself rushed into ruin.

But the soul was not made for scattering.  
It was made for order,  
for blessing,  
for a life gathered beneath God.

#### What This Vision Means

Immoderation is portrayed here as inner dispersion. The person of excess does not merely do too much; they lose their center. Their energies, appetites, words, and thoughts overflow their proper bounds until the self becomes scattered.

The fiery sea is an image of life without measure—vast, unstable, consuming. Sulfur reveals forgetfulness of God, because excess so often makes the soul inattentive. Fire signifies the destructive force of ignoring divine order.

One of the most striking lines is that immoderation belongs neither fully in heaven nor on earth. It cannot rest. It cannot be content. It keeps reaching, expanding, pushing, trying to experience everything, control everything, or indulge everything.

Obedience, then, is not presented as oppression but as rescue. Holy limits gather the soul back together. Measure is not lifelessness. It is the condition for peace.

#### Living This Vision

Immoderation does not always look dramatic.

Sometimes it is simply too much.  
Too much talking.  
Too much scrolling.  
Too much food.  
Too much reaction.  
Too much inward noise.

The deeper question is not how much you consume,  
but whether your life still has an inner center.

Do your habits gather you,  
or scatter you?

This vision calls us back to measure.  
Not as punishment,  
but as mercy.

Choose one limit that restores peace.  
Stop before the overflow.  
Leave some space unfilled.

The soul needs boundaries  
the way a flame needs a lamp.

Without form, fire only spreads.

Ask not only,  
“What am I allowed to do?”  
but also,  
“What helps me remain whole?”

A measured life is not small.  
It is spacious enough for God.
