## Day 127 - The Slippery Sandals of Lust

> *I saw lust reclining as though at ease, yet all around it was drying into ruin. It promised pleasure and freedom, but its path was too slick to stand in and too empty to sustain life.*

Then I was shown the meaning  
of the seventh image.

It is Lust.

It comes after despair,  
for when people lose hope in the mercy of God,  
they often turn downward toward the flesh,  
clinging to whatever pleasure can still be taken,  
as though bodily satisfaction  
might fill the place where trust has died.

It had an almost feminine form,  
for as woman is made for bearing children,  
so lust becomes the craving  
and encouragement of sin.

It lay on its right side,  
because it belittles righteousness  
and the chaste works that are good.

Sometimes it speaks of God  
and tries to excuse itself.  
Sometimes it claims it wishes to repent,  
yet it does not repent.  
It rests instead in false security.

It drew its legs up toward itself  
as one resting in bed.

For lust bends the strength  
that should rise toward God,  
and drags it down  
into the fragility and twisting of the flesh.

It numbs the mind with voluptuous softness,  
until through the mouths of the lustful it says:  
“I cannot abstain.  
I am too bound to my own desire.”

Its hair was like flames of fire,  
because it turns modesty—  
which should incline the mind toward chastity—  
into the heat of appetite,  
feeding itself and others  
with dangerous flames.

Its eyes were white as chalk,  
for it does not want to look on what is demanding.  
It leans toward an impure laziness,  
and so becomes even more ready  
for the cravings of lust.

Its sandals were white, smooth, and slippery,  
so that it could neither walk nor stand.

For lust imagines pleasure to be beautiful,  
and the mortification of the flesh to be unfair.  
So it makes foolish and lewd gestures,  
unable to walk in the honor of righteousness  
or stand in the meekness of stability.

It runs down every shameful path,  
sometimes leaping upward,  
yet often falling into the abyss.

From its mouth came poisonous slime,  
for lust boasts in filthy words  
and teaches what it desires through corruption.

At one breast it nursed a creature like a dog,  
and at the other something like a serpent,  
for where wisdom should be nourished,  
it feeds folly,  
and where bitterness should be restrained,  
it lets it grow.

It tore flowers and herbs from trees and meadows  
and smelled them with delight,  
for lust persuades the undiscerning  
to pluck both natural and unnatural pleasures  
and savor their fragrance  
until they think they cannot live without them.

It was naked, burning,  
and dried all beside it like hay,  
for lust strips away modesty and honesty  
and consumes the fruit of holiness  
in all who come near its flame.

But Chastity stood against it,  
calling the soul back  
to freshness, honesty, and holy rest.

#### What This Vision Means

This vision shows lust as a distortion of desire after hope has collapsed. When a person no longer expects good from God, bodily appetite can begin to feel like the only remaining comfort. Lust then becomes not merely a temptation, but a counterfeit refuge.

The imagery is exact. The reclining posture shows indulgence. The drawn-up legs suggest energy turned inward and downward rather than upward toward God. The fiery hair reveals passion inflamed without order. The chalk-white eyes and slippery sandals show something equally important: lust does not want the labor of love, discipline, or truth. It prefers ease, but cannot stand securely in it.

The creatures at the breasts suggest the nurturing of distorted affections—folly, venom, and unclean instinct. The plucked flowers and herbs show how lust handles beauty: it does not protect or reverence it, but tears it free for consumption.

Chastity is not the enemy of delight. It is the guardian of delight. It keeps desire truthful, ordered, and alive before God.

#### Living This Vision

Lust often begins where hope has thinned.

When the heart is lonely,  
tired, ashamed, or spiritually numb,  
the body’s cravings can feel louder than truth.

This vision invites compassion and honesty.  
Not panic.  
Not denial.  
But clear seeing.

Ask yourself:  
What am I really seeking right now?  
Comfort? Escape? Relief? Control?  
Am I asking desire to carry  
what only God can heal?

Chastity is not merely saying no.  
It is learning to love rightly.  
It is refusing to turn beauty into appetite  
and another person into use.

When desire rises,  
slow down.  
Do not immediately feed it.  
Bring your body back into prayer.  
Walk. Breathe. Tell the truth.  
Ask for fresh water, not poisoned sweetness.

What lust dries out,  
God can make living again.
